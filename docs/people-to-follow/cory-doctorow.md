# Cory Doctorow

*Author and activist who popularized "reverse centaur" for AI deployments that subordinate the human to the machine. Public-teaching source on the labor and capital failure modes of the AI bubble.*

---

## Background

Doctorow is a science-fiction novelist, journalist, and longtime technology-and-labor critic. He writes prolifically on platform decay (he coined "enshittification"), monopoly, and the political economy of automation. His commentary is principal-facing and macro: who captures the value of a technology, who absorbs its harms, and whether the business model underneath actually returns capital.

## Why he is on this wiki

Doctorow is not an applied-AI implementer. He is on this wiki because he names the deployment anti-pattern an implementer must refuse to build: the [reverse centaur](/concepts/reverse-centaur), where the human is paced by the machine, deskilled, and positioned to absorb blame. The implementer who internalizes the frame designs engagements that keep the human in charge of the decision. The implementer who ignores it ships rubber-stamp oversight and accountability sinks, then watches the value evaporate.

His critique is the labor-and-capital mirror of what the disciplines on this wiki teach as craft. Reading him keeps the implementer honest about what a deployment is actually doing to the people inside it.

## Field Notes

### 2026-06-26: "There Are Reasons to Be Optimistic About the A.I. Bubble Bursting" (Slate, video interview)

The interview that seeded the contribution above. Three moves worth lifting:

1. **Centaur vs reverse centaur.** A centaur is a human assisted by a machine that moves faster than they can but does not tell them what to do. The reverse centaur inverts control: the machine paces the human, and the human serves it.
2. **The accountability sink.** The reverse-centaur business case is to fire most of a team, keep a fraction to "mark the AI's homework," and make them bear the blame when it fails. The remaining worker carries fault for a system they do not control.
3. **Automation blindness.** Reduce the human to clicking "okay" on output that is usually right, and they go inured and stop checking. When it is finally wrong, their signature is on it. The deployment relocates failure rather than removing it, which is why a reverse-centaur strategy cannot return capital the way its backers need.

> "A centaur in automation theory is a person assisted by a machine. You're a human in control of a machine. The machine can do things you can't do, move faster than you, but it doesn't tell you what to do. The reverse [centaur is when] you're firing nine tenths of your workers, putting the remaining tenth in charge of marking the AI's homework, and then making them bear the blame when things go wrong."
>
> Cory Doctorow, ["There Are Reasons to Be Optimistic About the A.I. Bubble Bursting"](https://youtu.be/r03DPWGIxfY), Slate, 2026-06-26

Where it landed on this wiki: [Reverse Centaur](/concepts/reverse-centaur) as a concept page; cross-linked from [The Jagged Frontier](/perspectives/the-jagged-frontier) as the inversion of Mollick's centaur pattern.
