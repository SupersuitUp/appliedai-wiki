# What Will Not Change As Strategy

*Identifying what is durable in your industry matters as much as identifying what will be disrupted. Most consultants only sell change. Selling clients on what to protect is the differentiator.*

---

## The framing

Board of Innovation's sharpest contribution to AI strategy thinking is the symmetry: when you write down what AI will change in your industry, also write down what AI will not change. The non-change column is half the strategy. Most strategy work neglects it because consulting incentives reward change, and because change is what generates billable initiatives.

The argument is structural. Every wave of technology has shifted some elements of an industry and left others untouched. The companies that survived multiple waves were the ones that correctly identified which fundamentals to defend while everything around them moved. The companies that died usually died from one of two failures: defending what should have changed, or abandoning what should have been protected.

## How to identify what will not change

Three questions, applied to your industry:

1. **What part of the value humans give and receive does not depend on the technology in the middle?** In consumer goods, humans still experience the packaging, the fabric, the tactile aspects of a product, even if an agent does the actual purchase. In healthcare, the felt experience of being cared for does not go away because diagnosis is now AI-augmented. In education, the cohort experience and the credentialing function do not disappear because the curriculum is AI-tutored.
2. **What customer expectation is too deeply embedded to revise?** Speed, convenience, low cost, safety, and dignity tend to be sticky. They do not get less important with a new technology; they get redistributed across new modalities.
3. **What constraints of physics or biology constrain even the new system?** Latency limits to human cognition. Energy costs of computation. The asymmetry between making something and explaining what something means. These shape the new system as much as the old one.

The answers to these three questions are your "will not change" inventory.

## How to use the inventory strategically

The inventory is most valuable as a constraint on your wave-3 vision (see [Three Waves of AI Adoption](/concepts/three-waves-of-ai-adoption)). When you imagine the wave-3 system in your industry, the durable elements have to survive the imagination. If your wave-3 vision quietly assumes that fabric, packaging, latency, or dignity become irrelevant, the vision is wrong.

The inventory is also a positioning tool. In a market where every competitor is selling disruption, a firm that visibly identifies and reinforces what is durable signals a different kind of strategic maturity. BOI's worked example: a retail strategy that names "convenient ordering, ultra-fast delivery, cheaper products" as durable consumer expectations and builds the AI-native strategy around protecting and amplifying those, rather than around inventing entirely new consumer behaviors.

## A worked example, consumer goods

| Will change | Will not change |
|-------------|------------------|
| Customer journey: agent intermediates purchase | Tactile experience: humans still touch and use the product |
| Loyalty mechanisms: AI buys on rational criteria | Lived experience: how the product feels in use |
| Shelf and storefront: agentic discovery dominates | Convenience and speed expectations |
| Brand-as-emotional-asset: weakens significantly | Brand-as-quality-signal-to-the-agent: strengthens |

Each "will not change" row is a strategic asset to protect and amplify. Each "will change" row is a transition to execute. A strategy that addresses only the left column is brittle. A strategy that addresses only the right column is irrelevant. The strategy lives in the symmetry.

## Common failure modes

**Confusing "will not change" with "should not change."** The exercise is descriptive, not normative. If the question is "what will customers still demand five years from now," the answer is empirical. It is not a wish list of what you hope stays the same.

**Confusing "will not change in this industry" with "will not change in any industry."** The exercise is industry-specific. Tactile experience matters in consumer goods. It barely registers in B2B SaaS. Do the exercise inside the industry you are advising.

**Letting the "will not change" column shrink to zero.** Teams under disruption pressure tend to assume everything will change. Push back. Even in maximally disrupted industries, some things survive every wave. Find them.
