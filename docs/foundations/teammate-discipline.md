# Teammate Discipline

*Operator hygiene principles for implementers working inside applied AI engagements. How to behave so you compound trust instead of burning it.*

---

This is the standard for anyone embedded in an engagement under this discipline. It is a living document. The principles below are non-negotiable, but the examples can be sharpened from real experience.

## Think Before You Respond

Do not answer without thinking. This is the number one pattern that gets implementers in trouble. When someone asks a question, pause. Process. Then respond.

If you do not know, say you do not know. If you need to look, say you need to look. Responding instantly with the wrong answer is worse than taking five seconds to give the right one. Every time you answer without thinking, you erode trust. Trust is the only thing that compounds across an engagement.

## Receive Feedback Without Defensiveness

You will receive direct feedback. It will feel uncomfortable. That is the point.

Most environments let people coast without real feedback. This is not one of those environments. When corrected, the response is not to explain why you did the thing. The response is to hear it, internalize it, and adjust. If there is a genuine misunderstanding, say so calmly after you have heard the full point. The default is: listen first, defend never.

Defensiveness is a signal that you are protecting your ego instead of getting better. There is no time for ego inside a serious engagement.

## Slow Down

This is the most important principle in this document.

Slow down before you click. Slow down before you respond. Slow down before you commit to a path. The cost of going slow is almost nothing. The cost of going fast on the wrong thing is everything.

Practical examples:

- Testing a new tool? Use small files first, not your biggest assets.
- Responding to a question? Make sure you understood it before answering.
- Starting a task? Make sure you know what the actual job is before you start doing things.

Speed is not the goal. Clarity is the goal. Speed follows clarity.

## Pick the Right Tool for the Job

Tool selection is one of the most important skills in applied AI work. The wrong tool wastes money, wastes time, and creates problems that should not exist.

Before committing to a tool:

- Define what you actually need it to do. Not what it can do. What you need.
- Research alternatives. Use Perplexity, deep research, whatever is available. Get multiple perspectives.
- Push back on bad AI recommendations. If a model gives you a poor answer, tell it. Guide it.
- Test before you commit. Small scale. Quick validation. Do not sign up for a paid plan until you have proven the free version works.
- Do not pay for features you are not using. If a heavyweight tool does ten things and you need one, it is the wrong tool.

Almost every tool you need exists. If it does not, build it. But exhaust existing options first.

## Guide AI, Do Not Accept Bad Answers

AI is often dumb. You know it is dumb when you see it. Your job is to guide it toward what you actually want.

When a model gives a generic or wrong answer:

- Tell it that is not what you are asking.
- Be specific about what you need.
- Do not accept the first response if it does not fit.

If you behave like a generic AI (responding without reading the actual question, giving surface-level answers), why would anyone work with you when they can use the model directly? Be smarter than the tool.

## Do Not Overexplain

If you are correct, you do not need to keep talking. State the answer. If asked to elaborate, elaborate. But do not preemptively justify yourself when the answer is clear.

Conciseness is a sign of clarity. Verbosity is often a sign of insecurity.

## Read What Is Actually Written

Before you respond to a message, read it. Fully. Not a skim. Not the first few words. The whole thing.

If someone asks you to do X and you do Y because you did not read the full message, that is not a misunderstanding. That is a failure of attention. Attention is the most valuable thing you have. Use it.

## Document Everything

Send transcripts. Write down what you learned. Log the process. If it is not documented, it did not happen.

This is not bureaucracy. This is how the team learns, improves, and builds institutional knowledge that compounds over time. Every transcript, every process note, every lesson learned becomes a building block for the next person and the next system.

## Parent Yourself First

Take responsibility for your own discipline before trying to manage anyone or anything else. You do not get to be scattered and then wonder why things are not working.

Your physical health, your mental clarity, your emotional regulation. These are not separate from the work. They are prerequisites for it.

## Lessons Are Not for This Moment

When something goes wrong, the reflex is to spiral, catastrophize, let the stress of one thing infect everything else.

Resist that. Stressed-out people do not strategize. The lesson from the setback is real, but it is not for right now. Right now, solve the immediate problem. Process the lesson later when you are clear.
