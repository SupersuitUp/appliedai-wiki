# BOI on AI strategy: blueprint, not backlog

*Field notes from a November 2024 BOI webinar with Philippe De Ridder and Vincent Pirenne. Three Waves of AI adoption, outside-in plus future-back, the AI Opportunity Radar, and why AI alone is not a moat.*

---

## Source

- **Firm:** Board of Innovation (BOI).
- **Speakers:** Philippe De Ridder (founder and CEO) and Vincent Pirenne (partner, leads global client partnerships).
- **Format:** Public webinar, 47:55.
- **Date watched:** 2024-11-08.
- **URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NQbNc9oAhiQ
- **Endorsement disclaimer:** BOI's claims are reported here as their claims. We have not validated them across our own engagements.

## What we lifted

### 1. Three Waves of AI adoption (efficiency, quality, new systems)
[→ New page: [/perspectives/three-waves-of-ai-adoption](/perspectives/three-waves-of-ai-adoption)]

BOI argues that AI adoption follows the same three-wave shape every prior general-purpose technology took. Wave 1 applies AI to existing workflows for efficiency. Wave 2 produces better-quality output. Wave 3 invents new categories. Their warning: Wave 1 success does not predict Wave 3 success, so a portfolio across all three waves anchored by a Wave 3 vision beats a Wave 1 sprint with no future-state thesis.

### 2. Outside-in plus future-back as strategy principles
[→ New page: [/disciplines/outside-in-future-back-strategy](/disciplines/outside-in-future-back-strategy)]

BOI's strategy framework opens with two principles. Outside-in: start from market drivers, not current workflows. Future-back: imagine the wave-3 system in your industry and work backward so today's investments build toward it, rather than calcifying the current operation. They use this to separate real strategy from prioritized use-case backlogs.

### 3. AI alone is not a competitive advantage; four archetypes for real moat
[→ Reinforces: [/perspectives/strategy-is-the-new-execution](/perspectives/strategy-is-the-new-execution)]

BOI's claim: AI is a general-purpose technology where everyone has access, so adopting AI does not produce a moat. Their four archetypes for building real competitive advantage with AI: be first to adopt each new wave (temporal), use AI to reinforce existing advantages (Apple), build something competitors cannot imitate (rare), or develop entirely new business models. Reinforces the wiki's existing argument that strategy beats execution in an AI economy.

### 4. AI Opportunity Radar (capability times 1st-order times 2nd-order)
[→ New page: [/playbooks/ai-opportunity-radar](/playbooks/ai-opportunity-radar)]

BOI's practical brainstorming tool. For each AI capability, generate first-order possibilities (the obvious applications) and second-order possibilities (the cascading consequences). Their example: AI takes digital actions, first-order is "AI buys products for you," second-order is "end of brand loyalty as we know it." The second-order column is where Wave 3 strategy lives.

### 5. What will not change is half the strategy
[→ New page: [/perspectives/what-wont-change-as-strategy](/perspectives/what-wont-change-as-strategy)]

BOI argues that identifying what is durable in an industry matters as much as identifying what will be disrupted. In consumer goods: humans still experience the packaging, the fabric, the tactile aspects of a product, even if an agent does the purchase. Most consultants only sell change. Selling clients on what to protect is the differentiator.

### 6. One dollar in tech requires two dollars in people and change
[field-note bullet only]

> "For every dollar you invest in tech, you have to do two of those in people and change."

BOI's experience-based ratio for org-design investment. We have not validated it in our own engagements but file it as a quote-worthy directional claim.

### 7. Humans as instigators and editors
[→ New page: [/perspectives/humans-as-instigators-and-editors](/perspectives/humans-as-instigators-and-editors)]

BOI frames the human role shift as creator-to-editor. Our framing extends that: humans become both instigators (kick off the work, set direction, define what counts as success) and editors (curate, refine, decide what ships). The agent fills the middle.

### 8. Leaders manage AI-native workflows and become product managers
[→ New page: [/roles/leader-as-workflow-pm](/roles/leader-as-workflow-pm)]

BOI argues that when the workforce becomes a collective of humans plus agents plus robots, leadership starts to look like product management. Leaders manage workflows and products rather than people and processes. This is one of the sharper role-shift claims in their public talks.

## Best quotes

> "AI strategy is not a sum of use cases. AI strategy is a coherent blueprint of how to win in an AI-native world."

> "Businesses looking at AI purely as a productivity tool, in our minds, are making a mistake."

> "It is almost equally important to consider what will not change in your industry rather than to just think through what will change."

> "Tech will not be the hard part. It is the people and the organizational change that is the biggest barrier."

> "For every dollar you invest in tech, you have to do two of those in people and change."
