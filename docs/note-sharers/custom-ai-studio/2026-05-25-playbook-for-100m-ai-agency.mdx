# The Playbook for a $100M AI Agency

**Source:** Custom AI Studio (Devin Karns, co-founder/CEO), interviewed by Nate Herk · 1h 44m podcast
**Watched:** 2026-05-25

Devin Karns runs Custom AI Studio, a two-and-a-half-year-old AI consulting firm that has explicitly chosen the enterprise-value path over the lifestyle-business path. This episode is the most candid public articulation of that posture and the operating choices that flow from it. Quoted EBITDA multiples, named engagement-pricing tiers, concrete client P&L deltas. Worth treating as the canonical reference for the "are we building a small AI agency or a sellable services firm" question.

## What I lifted

### 1. Lifestyle vs. enterprise-value posture → [→ new perspective: /perspectives/lifestyle-or-enterprise-value-posture](/perspectives/lifestyle-or-enterprise-value-posture)
The most consequential structural choice an AI consulting founder makes happens in the first month and almost never gets named. Devin and his co-founder decided on day one whether they were building a lifestyle business or a business with meaty enterprise value and an exit plan. Most founders never name it, drift into a lifestyle shape by default, and then resent the ceiling later. The decision shapes hiring, pricing, contract size, and what kinds of clients to chase.

### 2. The $5M re-rating threshold → [→ new foundation: /perspectives/services-firm-re-rating-threshold](/perspectives/services-firm-re-rating-threshold)
Services firms re-rate exponentially around $5M annual revenue. Below it, the multiple is 1-2x EBITDA. Above it, the multiple is 4-5x. Devin's quote: "If you're making $2M a year as a pure AI readiness consultant, you'll sell that business for probably $2M. But if you're making $6M a year, you can sell it for $30M." The number is the structural reason the lifestyle-vs-enterprise-value decision matters. A $4M agency is worth what a $2M agency is worth. A $6M agency is worth 15x more.

### 3. Mid-markets are the AI era's sweet spot → [→ new foundation: /perspectives/mid-markets-are-the-ai-sweet-spot](/perspectives/mid-markets-are-the-ai-sweet-spot)
Devin's most operationally specific take. SMBs and solo founders do not yet have the systematized decision trees and SOPs that AI systems get built from, so engagements with them turn into scope-creep passion projects with no through-line to P&L. Enterprises are too distributed to put together a holistic strategy and end up throwing bodies at problems instead. Mid-markets ($10M-$250M revenue) have had to hire offshore teams and write SOPs to survive, which means the logic of the business is already documented somewhere, which means an AI system can be built against it with a clear KPI and a clear benchmark. He frames it as "AI consulting infrastructure rich, ceiling not yet hit."

### 4. 97% of AI projects = converting business logic to AI system → [→ new concept: /concepts/convert-business-logic-to-ai-system](/concepts/convert-business-logic-to-ai-system)
The meta-pattern across every Custom AI Studio engagement, stated as a near-universal claim: "When you really break it down fundamentally, what we are doing is taking the logic of the business, whether they've written it down or not, and converting it into an AI system." Three implications follow: (a) the work is encoding, not invention; (b) the prerequisite is documented business logic, which is why mid-markets are the sweet spot; (c) the AI engineer's actual skill is reading the business logic out of an SME and rendering it in tools, not knowing the latest framework.

### 5. Clients are buying relief, not AI → [→ new perspective: /perspectives/buying-relief-not-ai](/perspectives/buying-relief-not-ai)
The single sharpest positioning shift in the episode. Mid-market executives are getting pressured by their board, their internal AI advocates, and their LinkedIn-posting competitors to have an AI strategy. They are not buying AI systems. They are buying a coherent narrative they can take to their board, their team, and themselves: "We have an AI strategy. It's real. It's future-proof. We're early." Devin's exact frame: "What they're buying is relief, the fact that they can say they have an AI strategy."

### 6. The eleven ways to monetize AI expertise → [→ new perspective: /perspectives/eleven-ways-to-monetize-ai-expertise](/perspectives/eleven-ways-to-monetize-ai-expertise)
Devin's working chart of how AI experts are making money in 2026, plotted on a technical-depth-and-delivery-complexity axis against contract size. The 11: creator with sponsorships, freelancer, solo advisory consulting, pure AI readiness consulting, workshops and training, automations within n8n, managed agents, vertical products, horizontal platforms, hardware, full-stack consulting firm, private equity. The chart's payload is that each model has a different ceiling and a different exit profile. Lifestyle businesses cluster on the left. Enterprise-value businesses cluster on the right.

### 7. Workshop → Blueprint → Project → Tech Partnership engagement ladder → [→ new playbook: /playbooks/workshop-blueprint-project-partnership-ladder](/playbooks/workshop-blueprint-project-partnership-ladder)
Custom AI Studio's actual four-step engagement structure. Each step a fixed price offer. Each step builds trust and reduces the prospect's perceived risk before the next step gets pitched. Workshop ($1-5k, one-hour landscape briefing + two-hour industry-specific application session) → Blueprint ($15-35k, discovery and architecture deliverable the client could take to any dev shop) → Custom Project (variable, the actual build) → AI Technology Partnership (budget + percentage of topline). This is the missing playbook for AI consultants currently stuck pitching $5k automation projects.

### 8. The AI tech partnership revenue model → [→ new playbook: /playbooks/ai-tech-partnership-revenue-model](/playbooks/ai-tech-partnership-revenue-model)
Custom AI Studio is starting to move clients onto a budget-plus-percentage-of-topline-revenue model borrowed from the growth-marketing partner playbook. The premise: when an AI system is doing what a 10-person team used to do at a fraction of the cost, time-and-materials pricing leaves enormous value on the table. Devin's quote: "It's almost as if I just handed you 10 employees that are a fraction of the cost of your current employees, work 24/7, and are super smart." The pricing has to reflect the actual value, and it has to align incentives, which is what performance-based partnerships do.

### 9. Framework-first positioning → [→ new perspective: /perspectives/framework-first-positioning](/perspectives/framework-first-positioning)
Productize your service as a named framework. The Big-4 pattern (McKinsey, BCG, Bain, PwC, Deloitte, KPMG) is the reference: every one of them publishes signature frameworks that anchor their consulting work. Devin's specific anti-pattern: "I'll build whatever you want" sounds accommodating but actually reads as not-standing-on-anything. Trust attaches to operators who have an opinion about how the work gets done. Custom AI Studio's signature framework is what they call the Agentic Operating System, which is just an event-driven harness that classifies inputs, routes them to deterministic workflows, and uses LLMs only when needed.

### 10. What won't change as the durable AI strategy → [→ reinforces: /perspectives/what-wont-change-as-strategy](/perspectives/what-wont-change-as-strategy)
Devin reinforces the existing wiki page. The Bezos question, applied to AI infrastructure: "What's not going to change rather than what will." His worked example: when frameworks evolve every four months, you can't bet your firm on the framework. Bet on what's not going to change, which is that businesses will pay for outcomes, will need someone to read their business logic out of an SME, and will trust frameworks over freelancers.

### 11. AI eats labor allocation, not just software allocation → [→ new foundation: /perspectives/ai-eats-labor-allocation](/perspectives/ai-eats-labor-allocation)
The unit-economics reframe. A $1M/month law firm allocates about $2k/month to software, of which a 10-seat SaaS captures about $100. The same firm allocates roughly $300k/month to labor. AI systems that replace labor are capturing some of that labor allocation, not the software allocation. The implication: an AI services firm operating against a single law firm can earn 300x what a SaaS company operating against the same firm earns. This re-rates everything about the services-vs-product debate that has dominated AI agency discourse.

### 12. The 1% margin problem at scale
**[bullet only]** A 1% error rate on 100 test runs reads as "within margin." At a daily production volume of 10,000 it becomes 200 errors per day, which is business-ending in most contexts. The implication: eval and QA discipline is not optional, "Claude said success" is not a green check, and the project manager has to know what success actually looks like in the domain to validate the output. This reinforces the broader eval/teammate discipline already covered by [Teammate Discipline](/disciplines/teammate-discipline) and [First Eval Harness](/playbooks/first-eval-harness): bullet here, no new page.
