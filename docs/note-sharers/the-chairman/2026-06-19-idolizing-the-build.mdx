# The arrogance of developers, and the token-max compulsion

*First-person fieldwork on why developers over-build solved infrastructure, why spending tokens feels like progress when it is not, and the identity shift from vibe coder to operator who buys what is already solved.*

---

## Source

- **Firm:** The Chairman.
- **Format:** Internal voice braindump.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19.
- **Endorsement disclaimer:** These are The Chairman's direct observations on developer discipline, including his own. Claims reflect live operator experience, not published research.

## What we lifted

### 1. The arrogance of developers: over-building when the thing already exists
[→ new page: [Idolizing the Build](/perspectives/idolizing-the-build)]

The core move is reflexive: a developer sees a problem and writes code, even when a paid product already solves it, integrated and maintained. The Chairman names it as arrogance and Dunning-Kruger combined. The developer falls in love with the act of creating and quietly loses interest in the outcome the creating was supposed to serve.

### 2. Token-max: spending money mistaken for making money
[→ new page: [Idolizing the Build](/perspectives/idolizing-the-build)]

The agentic harness turned the build compulsion into a dopamine loop. You watch tokens burn, you watch code appear, and your body reports progress. But the spend is not the point. You should be excited about making money, not spending it. A boss can even temporarily amplify the token burn because it looks like motion.

### 3. Most of the infrastructure is already built: the GoHighLevel case
[field-note bullet only]

A platform like GoHighLevel bundles calendar, phone number, email, AI phone agents, and chatbot, plus the integration of all of them, for ninety-nine dollars a month. The value is in the wiring between the pieces, which is exactly what is hard to assemble by hand. Rebuilding affiliate-CRM infrastructure from scratch is vanity, and you inherit the maintenance forever.

### 4. The identity shift: from vibe coder to operator
[field-note bullet only]

The cure is an identity shift away from "developer / vibe coder" toward "operator who does whatever ugly thing makes money and saves time." Part of the saving is code never written; the larger part is code never maintained. The realization is hard to reach without owning a business, because the protective incentive (reach the outcome the cheapest, fastest way) only shows up when the money is your own.

### 5. The "I rebuilt X from scratch" pattern is a tell, not a flex
[field-note bullet only]

The timeline pattern of "I reimplemented Y in a weekend" is impressive work with frequently zero business value, because a paid product already does the thing. People are getting addicted to coding: the compiling-code rush, the opening hit of spending tokens. Nobody should be proud to be on the hook for boilerplate other people already figured out.

## Best quotes

> "Why the fuck would you build? The arrogance of developers. The Dunning-Kruger of developers."

> "Token max and retardation, where you just feel like, because you're spending tokens, you're doing something. You shouldn't be excited about spending money. You should be excited about making money."

> "I want to have an identity shift away from developer / vibe coder to businessman that just does whatever is ugly, it makes money."

> "If so much of the infrastructure is already built out, I don't need to reinvent the wheel. I don't want to be maintaining codebases that are just boilerplate things that other people have already figured out."
