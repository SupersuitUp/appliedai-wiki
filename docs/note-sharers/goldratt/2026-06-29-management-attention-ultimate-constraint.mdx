# Goldratt: management attention is the ultimate constraint

*Field notes from Rami Goldratt's webinar carrying his father's final-years thesis. Management attention is the binding constraint of every organization, and three killers quietly waste it.*

---

## Why a 2018 operations talk sits on an applied-AI wiki

AI is a multiplier. It scales whatever input you point it at, which means its payoff is decided entirely by where you aim it. Theory of Constraints is the discipline of aiming: find the one thing that actually limits the system, then bend everything toward relieving it. Goldratt's late claim sharpens it further. In most organizations the binding constraint is the leader's own attention, so the highest-leverage thing AI can do is give that attention back. Read the lifts below as the operating logic underneath [you are the bottleneck](/perspectives/you-are-the-bottleneck) and [clear the bottleneck, don't chase the bubble](/perspectives/clear-the-bottleneck-dont-chase-the-bubble).

## What we lifted

### 1. Management attention is the ultimate constraint
[→ Reinforces: [/perspectives/the-moving-bottleneck](/perspectives/the-moving-bottleneck)]

A resource is "critical" when it is both essential to the goal and chronically scarce. Management attention is both: every organization needs it to win, and every manager feels short of it. Eli Goldratt's final-years claim is that this, rather than cash, market demand, or a particular machine, is the real constraint of an organization. It is leadership's capacity to get results while absorbing complexity, uncertainty, and competing stakeholders. Everything downstream is decided by where that attention lands.

### 2. Exceptional value is removing a significant limitation
[field-note bullet]

Goldratt's definition of value is a useful aiming device for the constraint: "exceptional value is created by removing a significant limitation in a way that was not possible before." Omron's home blood-pressure monitor removed the limitation that the only way to measure blood pressure was a trip to the doctor, and built a billion-dollar company on that single removal. Attention aimed at anything smaller than a real limitation is attention wasted.

### 3. Focus starts with what not to do
[field-note bullet]

Plot waste against the number of open assignments and you get a U-curve. Too few and the manager sits idle. Too many and the manager is overloaded, which is where most operators actually live. The first move toward focus is not picking where to aim. It is the courage to decide what to stop. In Goldratt's words, "focus starts with what not to do."

### 4. Attention killer #1: chasing symptoms instead of the core vicious cycle
[field-note bullet]

The tell is feeling like "a dog chasing its tail" while the problem only grows despite real effort. That pattern means a vicious cycle is regenerating the symptoms faster than you can patch them. His worked example is intergenerational poverty: dozens of well-meaning, individually good programs each attack one symptom (crime, dropout rates, unemployment) while the underlying cycle stays intact. The fix is to find the cycle, break one load-bearing arrow (give youth a real education-plus-career path so a poor childhood no longer locks in a poor adulthood), and watch the symptoms collapse together as the cycle turns virtuous.

### 5. Attention killer #2: living in a compromise instead of breaking the core conflict
[field-note bullet]

When a core conflict goes unsolved, the organization oscillates between two bad extremes and burns its attention fighting the fires the compromise throws off. His example is a branded-jewelry maker stuck in an endless fight over exchange terms with its retailers (swap unsold stock one-to-one and the brand loses money; demand three-to-one and the store chokes on dead inventory). Both sides were negotiating a compromise. Breaking the conflict instead, with a constant-display consignment model (swap any piece any day, as long as the store holds a fixed total display value and reorders as it sells), dissolved the tradeoff. Sales doubled within a year.

### 6. Attention killer #3: chasing targets instead of building the enablers
[field-note bullet]

Under short-term pressure to hit a number, managers take distorted actions that dig the hole deeper. A chilled-pastry brand, hit by a cheaper retailer private label, cut its marketing (less awareness, sales kept dropping) and then cut the meat out of the product (erasing the very differentiation that justified its price). Targets are outputs. Attention belongs on the inputs that produce them. Goldratt's father's line: "when you are in a hole, the first thing you do is stop digging."

### 7. The five focusing steps, pointed at attention
[field-note bullet]

Theory of Constraints runs identify, exploit, subordinate, elevate, and then do not let inertia become the next constraint. Rami's emphasis is that with management attention the step that actually breaks is subordination: deciding what everything else must give up so the constraint can be exploited. It is the hardest and most-skipped step, and it is the same discipline as deciding what to stop doing.
