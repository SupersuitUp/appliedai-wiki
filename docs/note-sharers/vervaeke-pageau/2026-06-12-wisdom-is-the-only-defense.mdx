# The Wisdom Crisis: What LLMs Are Actually Doing to Us

**Source:** Transfigured (YouTube) · John Vervaeke, Jonathan Pageau, Sam (host, Google data scientist) · 1 hour 22 minutes
**Watched:** 2026-06-12

---

## What I lifted

### 1. AI-knowledge / AI-use inversion
[→ new page: [/perspectives/the-adoption-inversion](/perspectives/the-adoption-inversion)]

Peer-reviewed research shows strong negative correlation: the more people know about AI, the less they use it. The exact opposite of how people relate to every other tool in history. Vervaeke cites this as diagnostic of AI's ontological mismatch. Expertise with a tool normally produces more reliance on it. AI inverts the pattern. The implication: people who understand AI most have already encountered the classification wall that makes sustained confident use impossible.

### 2. AI is neither tool nor agent
[→ new page: [/concepts/ai-neither-tool-nor-agent](/concepts/ai-neither-tool-nor-agent)]

Vervaeke argued there is a growing branch of AI philosophy making the case that LLMs fit neither ontological category. A tool you master, use, and relate to instrumentally. An agent you are responsible to, who has interests and can refuse tasks. LLMs do neither: people form bizarre relationships to them (not tool behavior), but they have no genuine perspective, goals, or interests to be responsible to (not agent behavior). The classification failure produces behavioral failure: over-delegation on one side, overcrediting on the other.

### 3. Model collapse / tragedy of the commons
[→ reinforces: [/perspectives/slop-factory](/perspectives/slop-factory)]

AI trained on AI-generated content degrades: each iteration opens the gap between probabilistic term correlation and genuine relevance judgment. Vervaeke's analogy: photographing a photocopy of a photograph. Vibe coding accelerates this by enlarging the pool of AI-generated content available for training. Pageau drew the parallel to Larry Ellison's concern about the same dynamic (noted publicly). The tragedy of the commons: each individual AI use makes short-term sense; collectively the training corpus degrades for everyone. This is the macro-civilizational version of what [slop factory](/perspectives/slop-factory) describes at the business level.

### 4. LLMs have plateaued
[→ reinforces: [/perspectives/milking-humanity](/perspectives/milking-humanity)]

Vervaeke reports a growing consensus among AI leaders that LLMs will not get to general AI: "That is now moving to becoming an uncontroversial consensus amongst many of the leaders of the field." Supporting evidence: 50% of planned data center builds canceled or put on hold, models being watered down as cost recovery fails, trillions spent with no proportionate economic benefit. He also notes that as labs try to price AI at its actual cost, users drop off, forcing further model degradation to reduce costs. This provides empirical grounding for the overclaiming critique in [Milking Humanity](/perspectives/milking-humanity): the bill is arriving.

### 5. The Moloch trap / AI as idol
[→ new page: [/perspectives/ai-as-idol](/perspectives/ai-as-idol)]

Pageau's most direct provocation: "We're treating humans as a way to get better AI. That's the way that the companies are doing it." He framed this as the ancient idol pattern: you build the idol to serve you, then end up serving the idol. The Moloch trap (the competitive dynamic that produces outcomes no individual actor chose) is the mechanism. No single actor decided to treat humans as training data for machines; the incentive structure produced it as an emergent outcome. The bureaucracy parallel: once an automated system develops internal logic, the humans inside it spend more energy maintaining the system than doing the work the system was built to support.

### 6. Vice is quantifiable, virtue is not
[→ new page: [/perspectives/vice-is-quantifiable](/perspectives/vice-is-quantifiable)]

The objective-function problem for AI wisdom: porn robots have a clear utility function (measurable behavioral signals). Wisdom sages do not. Even the best mathematical approximation of the good pixelates it, introducing exploitable gaps between the metric and the actual good. Vices are further easy to monetize because they do not require effort from the user: addiction pulls. Virtue requires concentration, aim, and sustained effort. Every subscription/retention model rewards the pull. This is why AI monetizes in vice before virtue, and why any AI tool that optimizes engagement should be treated as suspect by operators.

### 7. Cognitive and technical debt
[→ reinforces: [/perspectives/llm-psychosis](/perspectives/llm-psychosis)]

Two debt types compound. Cognitive debt: verified brain-connectivity research shows measurable degradation from chatbot use. Skill atrophy from AI-delegated tasks (the data-scientist anecdote: projects take a fraction of the time now, but the speaker can write code by hand more slowly than five years ago). Technical debt: AI writing "superhuman code" that no human can read, debug, or repair when it breaks. The combination produces what Vervaeke calls a "mirage of productivity": apparent gains now, deep capacity destruction later.

### 8. Alignment to ultimate reality
[field-note bullet]

Vervaeke's only positive proposal for AI alignment: "The only thing that could really work is if we could get them to align to God or ultimate reality or the true, the good, and the beautiful." Aligning AI to humans is doomed, he argues, because humans are not aligned to reality ourselves. The convergence proposal: at sufficient abstraction, relevancy curves converge. Individuals have different relevance patterns for their lives, but when tracking higher-order things, humans can find common ground. Whether that convergence is reachable computationally is an open question he calls a "dark horse" rather than a prediction.

### 9. Wisdom education as the only real defense
[→ new page: [/perspectives/wisdom-as-the-response](/perspectives/wisdom-as-the-response)]

The convergence point of the conversation. Vervaeke: "The philosophical project, meaning philosophy as a way of life, the cultivation of virtue and wisdom, has never been more needed and has never been more under threat by the same phenomena." Pageau: "Everything else will be can be automated. So being a real human will become an adventure." Both argued that classical formation, not regulation, is the practical response. Not because AI can be stopped but because the people capable of using AI well, evaluating its outputs, and not becoming dependent on it are the ones who were formed before they were handed the tool. The post-AI version of this is Pageau's Ralston College argument: small communities forming humans in wisdom and virtue, so that when the AI infrastructure degrades, humans have the internal capacity to navigate what comes next.

### 10. Flynn effect reversal
[field-note bullet]

IQ has dropped for the first time in recorded history. The long-standing Flynn effect (steady population IQ growth) has reversed. Measurable decline started around 2013, coinciding with widespread smartphone and social media adoption. LLMs are accelerating the decline. Vervaeke cites this as bonafide research, not opinion. The implication for operators: within one generation, users may lack the internal cognitive formation to evaluate whether AI outputs are hallucinating.

### 11. Short-term power / long-term soul trade (pharmakeia)
[→ new page: [/concepts/pharmakeia-of-ai](/concepts/pharmakeia-of-ai)]

Pageau's structuring frame: all technology follows the pharmakeia pattern (Greek: medicine/poison). Short-term extension of human power comes at the cost of long-term atrophy of the internal capacity the extension replaces. Books extend memory, atrophy recall. AI extends cognition, atrophies the cognitive muscles it replaces. The "sorcery pattern" is pharmakeia past the balance point: you sell your soul (long-term capacity) for short-term power. All the folk stories of deals with the devil follow the same structure. The question for operators is not whether there is a trade but whether you are managing it deliberately.
