# Knowing AI Changes How You Use It

*The more someone knows about AI, the less they use it. The exact opposite of every other tool in human history. This inversion is one of the most diagnostic signals about what AI actually is.*

---

## The pattern

A peer-reviewed study found a strong negative correlation between AI knowledge and AI usage: the more people know about AI, the less they use it. John Vervaeke, citing this research in a June 2026 discussion, called it one of the most telling empirical signals about the nature of LLMs.

With every other tool, the relationship runs the other direction. You invest in learning the tool. You practice with it. Mastery makes you more capable and more reliant on it. Power tools, spreadsheets, IDEs, search engines: expertise and adoption move together.

LLMs break this pattern cleanly. The people who understand them most, use them least.

## Why this matters

Two readings are possible.

The first: experts simply know the failure modes. They know hallucination rates, know where LLMs confidently produce wrong answers, know the gap between fluent output and accurate output. Awareness of the failure modes reduces the value of the tool in contexts where accuracy matters.

The second: something deeper. Vervaeke's framing is that LLMs are [neither tool nor agent](/concepts/ai-neither-tool-nor-agent). People who most understand AI have absorbed enough to know the current category-frame does not fit. You cannot master a thing you cannot properly categorize. Disuse follows from ontological confusion more than from mere caution.

The two readings compound: if AI is genuinely hard to classify, and if misclassifying it leads to misuse, then the people who have most tried to use it carefully may have hit the classification wall first.

## The operator implication

The adoption inversion does not argue for non-use. It argues for epistemic humility about what you are actually working with.

The correct posture for an operator: treat AI outputs as drafts requiring discrimination, not as work product requiring sign-off. The [generate vs discriminate](/perspectives/llm-psychosis) frame is upstream of everything. Fluency is not accuracy. Volume is not value.

The inversion also undermines "just use it more" as a productivity prescription. If increased usage negatively correlates with knowledge, then the most productive relationship with AI may be one of structured, discriminating use, not frictionless delegation.

> **The tool you cannot categorize is the one most likely to be using you.**
