The lowest-trust positioning available to any consultant is "I'll build whatever you want." It sounds humble. It sounds accommodating. It reads, in the prospect's gut, as evidence that you do not have a strong opinion about how the work should be done, and therefore that you probably do not know how the work should be done. The fix is to productize your service into a named framework with a strong point of view about the shape of the engagement, and to lead with the framework, not with the build.

## What the pattern is

Every successful consulting firm at scale runs on signature frameworks. McKinsey has the 7S Framework. BCG has the Growth-Share Matrix. Bain has the Net Promoter Score methodology. Deloitte and PwC and KPMG each have dozens. These frameworks do four jobs at once:

1. **They productize the service.** A framework gives the consulting work a name and a shape that the prospect can hold in their head. The engagement becomes a thing instead of a billable-hours blob.
2. **They are the trust artifact.** A named framework signals that the consultant has thought about the problem deeply enough to have arrived at a structured approach. That signal is worth more than any case study.
3. **They are the differentiator.** Every consultant in your category does some version of the same work. The framework is what makes you the consultant who runs *this* version of the work.
4. **They are the cascade artifact.** The client's leadership team can re-explain the framework internally. It travels through the organization without you.

## Why "I'll build whatever you want" loses

Devin Karns of Custom AI Studio frames this directly: "What you run into as a vendor or dev shop is yeah, I'll just do whatever you want, you know, like just tell me and I'll build it. People would think that engenders trust. It actually quite does the opposite, because you're not standing on anything. And when you're not standing on anything, you don't feel as capable."

The dynamic is the same one that produces the "great kid, but I'm not signing" outcome in sales. A young rep who is friendly, eager, and accommodating gets liked but does not get the deal. A more senior rep who pushes back, asks harder questions, and disagrees on small things gets the deal. The difference is **conviction**. Buyers buy from operators who have a point of view they would defend.

A vendor without a framework cannot have a defensible point of view, structurally, because they have already signaled they will adapt to whatever the buyer wants. The buyer extracts maximum flexibility on the contract and then signs with someone else, or signs with the vendor at a lower price than the framework-first competitor would have commanded.

## What a signature framework looks like

A good consulting framework has three properties:

### 1. A named shape

The framework has a name that fits in a sentence. Not "our approach to AI implementation." Just **the Agentic Operating System** (Custom AI Studio's framework), or **the Applied AI Maturity Curve**, or **the Three Waves Adoption Map** (see [Three Waves of AI Adoption](/perspectives/three-waves-of-ai-adoption)). The name is the handle the client carries away from the meeting.

### 2. A structured methodology

The framework has a discrete number of steps or components, each with a clear purpose. Five steps is the conventional sweet spot. Three is acceptable for simple frameworks. More than seven is too many. The structure has to be memorable enough that the consultant can draw it on a whiteboard from memory and the client can re-draw it for their team a week later.

### 3. A defensible point of view

The framework asserts something. It is opinionated about how the work should be done, which means it implies other ways the work should not be done. The point of view is the trust artifact. A framework that "could apply to anything" applies to nothing in particular and produces no signal.

## How to develop your own framework

You probably already have a framework. You just have not named it yet. The fastest path to a signature framework is to:

1. **List the last 10-20 engagements you have run.** Look at the actual sequence of work in each.
2. **Find the pattern.** There is almost certainly a 3-5 step sequence you run on every engagement, even when each engagement looks superficially different.
3. **Name the pattern.** Give the sequence a name. Give each step a name. Both names should be specific to your category and memorable.
4. **Articulate the point of view.** What does your framework assert that other approaches do not? Write that down as the one-sentence thesis.
5. **Produce the artifacts.** A diagram of the framework. A one-page explainer. A slide deck that walks through the framework with a worked example.

Once the framework exists, lead with it. Every sales call opens with the framework. Every proposal organizes around the framework. Every case study maps onto the framework's steps. Every team member can explain the framework consistently.

## Worked example

Custom AI Studio's signature framework is the Agentic Operating System. It is an opinionated architecture: an event-driven harness that classifies inputs, routes them to deterministic workflows, and uses LLMs only when needed. The framework asserts that "LLM as orchestrator" is the wrong shape for most production AI systems and "harness as orchestrator with LLMs as tools" is the right shape. This single assertion makes Custom AI Studio different from every "agent-builder" firm that takes the LLM-as-orchestrator default.

A prospect on a sales call with Custom AI Studio is not just buying AI consulting. They are buying the Agentic Operating System approach to AI consulting. That distinction is what justifies the higher price point and the longer-term commitment. Without the framework, they would be choosing between vendors on price.

## When to update the framework

A framework is durable but not eternal. The right cadence to revisit it is roughly annually. Three reasons to update:

- **A new step is consistently appearing in engagements** that is not in the framework. Add it.
- **A step in the framework is consistently being skipped.** Either it does not belong, or your engagements are missing it and you should pull them back to the framework.
- **The technical substrate has shifted.** The Agentic Operating System looked different in 2024 than it does in 2026. The framework's vocabulary has to keep up with the field.

Updating the framework is positive signal, not negative. It demonstrates that the consultant is still actively practicing rather than coasting on a single artifact.

## The voice-and-positioning correlate

Framework-first positioning is the consulting version of voice-and-positioning work in writing and creative output. Both rest on the same premise: trust attaches to operators with a recognizable, defensible point of view. The lowest-trust position is the one with no signature. The highest-trust position is the one where the work and the operator are inseparable.
