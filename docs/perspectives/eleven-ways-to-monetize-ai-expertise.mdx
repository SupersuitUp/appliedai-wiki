The most common reason an AI consultant ends up in the wrong business model is that they never named which model they were trying to operate. Devin Karns of Custom AI Studio identifies eleven distinct ones. Each lives somewhere along a two-axis chart: technical depth and delivery complexity on the x-axis, contract size on the y-axis. Each has a structurally different ceiling, business shape, and exit story.

## The eleven

In rough order of increasing technical complexity and contract size:

1. **Creator with sponsorships.** YouTube, X, newsletter. Monetize the audience via sponsorships, affiliate links, or paid courses. Solo. Low capital, high distribution leverage. Ceiling: roughly $1-3M annual revenue for a strong solo creator. Sells as a personal brand or merges with the next person's brand. Not really sellable as a discrete business.
2. **Freelancer.** One-off project work for direct clients. Low ceiling because the founder is the rate-limiter. Ceiling: $300-500k. Not sellable.
3. **Solo advisory consulting.** One-on-one strategic guidance, typically monthly retainers. Higher rate per hour than freelancing because the value is judgment rather than execution. Ceiling: $400-700k. Not sellable.
4. **Pure AI readiness consulting.** Workshops, audits, readiness assessments. Higher contract sizes because the buyer is mid-market or enterprise. Productizable. Ceiling: $2-3M. Sellable at 1-2x EBITDA.
5. **Workshops, training, enablement.** Productized education delivered to teams inside companies. Metagu of Australia is the canonical example with Microsoft Copilot training. Ceiling: $3-5M. Sellable.
6. **Automations within n8n or similar.** Build automation workflows for clients. The classic "AI automation agency" model. Mostly lifestyle business. Ceiling: $1-3M.
7. **Managed agents.** "My agent is a content creator. You pay me a license fee to use it." Recurring revenue, agent-as-employee framing. Higher ceiling than automation work. Ceiling: $3-10M depending on agent quality.
8. **Vertical AI products.** SaaS-shaped products solving a specific industry problem with AI. Hardest play. Less than 0.1% are successful or defensible over time. Big winners exit at $100M+. Most quietly die.
9. **Horizontal AI platforms.** General-purpose AI tooling. Even harder than vertical products. Mostly dominated by labs and platform companies. Don't compete here unless you are funded for it.
10. **Hardware.** AI-enabled physical products. Capital-intensive. Slow. Massive ceiling if it works. Mostly not realistic for an AI consultant.
11. **Full-stack consulting firm.** Discovery, architecture, build, deploy, maintain. The Big-4 shape, applied to AI. Where Custom AI Studio is positioning. Ceiling: $50M-$1B+. Sells at 4-5x EBITDA once across the [re-rating threshold](/perspectives/services-firm-re-rating-threshold).

Plus a twelfth that Devin includes as the outer-orbit option:

12. **Private equity / acquisition.** Buy small AI consulting firms, roll them up, sell the conglomerate. Different game entirely. Requires capital, M&A expertise, and a longer time horizon. Anthropic + Blackstone + Goldman just announced a $1.5B firm doing essentially this.

## The two axes

The x-axis (technical depth and delivery complexity) determines how hard the work is and how much defensibility you have. A creator with sponsorships has very low technical depth and very high commodity risk. A full-stack consulting firm has very high technical depth, high integration with client workflows, and high switching cost once embedded.

The y-axis (contract size) determines what a single client is worth and how many clients you need to hit a given revenue number. A freelancer at $5-20k per project needs a constant pipeline. A full-stack firm at $200k-$2M per partnership needs many fewer.

A useful diagnostic: where are you actually operating right now versus where you tell people you operate? Most operators describe themselves as the model one or two rungs above their actual model, which produces persistent disappointment.

## Where the models cluster

| Cluster | Models | Ceiling | Exit story |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lifestyle** | Creator, freelancer, solo advisory, automation agency | $300k - $3M | None or low multiple |
| **Productized lifestyle** | Workshops, training, managed agents | $3 - $10M | 1-2x EBITDA, sometimes |
| **Bet-the-ranch** | Vertical products, horizontal platforms, hardware | $0 or $100M+ | All or nothing |
| **Enterprise-value services** | Full-stack consulting, PE rollup | $50M - $1B+ | 4-5x EBITDA at scale, full-value exit |

The cluster determines the operating life as much as the financial outcome. A productized-lifestyle business is a calm operating year with reliable revenue and limited team management. A bet-the-ranch product company is a long set of high-stress years where most quietly die. An enterprise-value services firm is a slower compounding curve with a real exit at the end.

## How to pick

Three questions:

1. **What kind of working life do you want?** Most operators answer "I want to work for myself" and stop there. The follow-up matters: do you want to do the building, do you want to manage builders, or do you want to manage the people who manage builders?
2. **What is your distribution edge?** Creators have audience. Consultants have rolodex. Builders have references. Pick a model that compounds on the distribution edge you actually have, not the one you wish you had.
3. **What's the exit story you want?** Most operators don't have one. Naming it forces the upstream decisions.

The combination of answers usually points to one of the eleven. Pick it, commit to it, do not drift between two.

## Why the chart matters even if you don't pick a "big" model

Most readers of this wiki should probably pick from the lifestyle or productized-lifestyle clusters, not the bet-the-ranch or enterprise-value ones. That is the right answer for most operators, and the chart's value is not arguing otherwise. The chart's value is that knowing which model you are operating prevents you from accidentally drifting into a hybrid that has the worst characteristics of two.

A freelancer who tries to also build a product has the freelancer's revenue ceiling and the product company's stress profile. A creator who tries to also run a consulting practice splits their distribution attention and underdelivers on both. The hybrids are usually worse than either pure model. Pick.
