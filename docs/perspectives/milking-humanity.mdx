# Milking Humanity

*Overstating AI capability to drive share price is an ethical violation, not just a marketing mistake. The Faustian bargain: the lie must go all the way down, including into the lab's own culture, to maintain credibility.*

---

## The ethical claim

AI labs systematically overstate the current capability of their technology to investors, the press, and the public. The intended audience is the capital market. The side effect is that companies, CEOs, CTOs, and individual practitioners make decisions based on a false picture of what AI can actually do today.

That gap between the claimed capability and the actual capability carries real costs. Developers restructure their workflows, abandon craft depth, burn out, and feel existential dread about their profession, all in response to a narrative that serves the lab's fundraising position, not the practitioner's actual situation.

This is a moral problem. Not a marketing one.

## The Faustian bargain

The structure of the overclaiming creates an internal trap. If an AI lab says "coding is solved" publicly to boost its valuation, the lab cannot then have internal documents that say "actually, here is what we know the tool cannot do yet." The two positions are incompatible.

The result: to maintain external credibility, the lab must also propagate the inflated narrative internally. Engineers are incentivized to use the tool in ways that produce the metrics the narrative requires. The emperor's technology has no clothes, but calling it out means getting removed from the room.

The Faustian bargain is that you cannot stop the lie at the boundary of the press release. The organizational culture around the product becomes oriented toward preserving the narrative rather than improving toward it. The lab destroys the integrity of its internal feedback loops to sustain the stock price.

## The investor-practitioner split

The claims that make investors say "wow" are structurally different from the claims that help practitioners do real work. "8x the code shipped per employee" tells an investor a story about a step-change in productivity. It tells a practitioner almost nothing useful, because it does not address correctness, reliability, or the cost of the tail failures.

Labs choose the metric that serves the investor narrative. They deliver it to an audience that includes practitioners who are not the intended recipient. The practitioners absorb the harm of taking the message at face value. The lab does not.

## The scale of the harm

The harm is not abstract. It shows up as:

- Teams disbanding or downsizing based on an overstated automation capability
- Individual developers experiencing burnout and professional anxiety driven by a false competition narrative
- Companies building toward capabilities that do not yet exist and catching the cost when they fail
- Practitioners being told to run loops and let AI rip, producing code no one understands, in response to advice from people whose tokens cost nothing

The scale of the harm scales with the credibility of the source. When the most prominent AI labs in the world say "coding is solved," many people believe them. That credibility is the product of years of genuine technical progress. Using it to boost a short-term narrative is a specific kind of betrayal.

## What to do with this

Apply the same rigor to AI capability claims that you would apply to any claim made by a party with a financial interest in your believing it.

The correct response to genuine AI acceleration is to leverage it with informed judgment. The correct response to capability overclaiming is to probe the actual capability at the task level and build from what is demonstrably there.

The labs that will earn long-term trust are the ones that close the gap between what they claim and what their products do. Until that gap closes, practitioners should treat headline capability claims as investor communication and validate everything themselves.
