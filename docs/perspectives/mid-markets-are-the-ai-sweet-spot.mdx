*Mid-market companies ($10M-$250M annual revenue) are the AI era's prime client profile for consulting firms. SMBs lack the documented business logic AI systems get built from. Enterprises are too distributed to execute. Mid-markets are systematized just enough.*

The conventional wisdom in AI consulting circa 2026 says that solo founders and SMBs are the best clients (lowest friction, fastest decisions) and enterprises are the most lucrative (biggest budgets). Devin Karns's operating experience at Custom AI Studio contradicts both: the mid-market band is the sweet spot, by a meaningful margin, and the reason has nothing to do with budget. It has to do with whether the client has already done the prerequisite work that AI systems get built against.

## The argument

Every AI engagement is fundamentally the same exercise: take the logic of the business and convert it into an AI system (see [Convert Business Logic to AI System](/concepts/convert-business-logic-to-ai-system)). Whether the system is an automation, an agent, a chatbot, or a full operating layer, the substrate is the business's existing decision trees, SOPs, KPIs, and benchmarks. The quality of the AI system is bounded by the quality of that substrate.

**SMBs and solo founders typically have no substrate.** They are too small to have written down their processes. Decisions live in the founder's head. KPIs are loose. There is no benchmark to judge an AI system against. Engagements with SMBs turn into scope-creep passion projects with no through-line to P&L. The AI consultant ends up doing both the discovery work (figuring out what the business does) and the build work (encoding it into a system), at a price point that does not justify the discovery cost.

**Enterprises ($1B+) have substrate but cannot execute against it.** Enterprises are systematized in every individual function, but the functions are siloed, the strategy is fragmented across business units, and the procurement cycle is long enough that the model has shifted twice before the contract is signed. Devin's exact line: enterprises are "ironically less systematized than mid-markets in a funny way because they just throw bodies at problems." Holistic AI transformation is structurally hard at enterprise scale.

**Mid-markets ($10M-$250M ARR) are the sweet spot.** They have grown past the point where the founder remembers everything. They have hired offshore teams, which forced them to write SOPs. They have departments with named KPIs that are tied to the P&L. The decision trees exist, often on paper. The benchmark exists. An AI system can be built against documented logic, with a clear definition of success, and shipped before the model shifts again.

## Why mid-market AI engagements succeed

Three structural reasons:

1. **Documented business logic.** The mid-market client has already paid the cost of writing things down. The AI engagement starts from a higher floor.
2. **Real KPIs with real money attached.** An e-commerce mid-market with 25 products and a 21% refund rate can quantify a 1% improvement in refund rate as millions of dollars. SMBs cannot quantify their wins at that resolution. Enterprises can, but the quantification is fragmented across 40 dashboards owned by 40 different teams.
3. **A decision maker who feels the value.** Mid-market CEOs and COOs are close enough to operations to see the impact of an AI system on the next week's numbers. Enterprise decision makers are insulated from operations by layers of management.

The combination produces engagements where the AI system clearly works, the value is clearly attributed, and the next engagement gets sold off the back of the first. The flywheel is built into the client profile.

## The revenue band

| Annual revenue | Profile | AI engagement viability |
|---|---|---|
| < $1M | Solo / SMB | Founder is the substrate. Engagements drift into passion projects. |
| $1M - $10M | Small business / lower SMB | Some SOPs exist, mostly in the founder's head. Engagements work if scoped tight. |
| **$10M - $50M** | **Lower mid-market** | **Sweet spot. SOPs exist. Decision tree is documented. KPI is clear.** |
| **$50M - $250M** | **Upper mid-market** | **Sweet spot. Multiple functions to attack. Larger contracts.** |
| $250M - $1B | Lower enterprise | Departments are now their own businesses. Sales cycles lengthen. |
| > $1B | Enterprise | Procurement is its own department. Strategy is fragmented. |

## How to find them

Mid-markets do not advertise themselves the way SMBs do. The founder is not on Twitter every day. They are not paying for ads in your feed. They are growing through customer relationships and word-of-mouth referrals. Three sourcing channels work:

1. **Referral from peer service providers.** Their lawyer, their accountant, their growth-marketing partner. Mid-market CEOs trust other professionals.
2. **Industry trade events.** Not the AI ones. The category events for whatever vertical your target works in.
3. **Outbound to specifically-named people.** A mid-market CEO will respond to a thoughtful email if the email demonstrates you understand their specific industry. Mass outbound does not work; targeted-by-name outbound does.

## The pricing implications

Mid-market clients can pay six-figure annual contracts comfortably. The $100k-$1M range is where most Custom AI Studio mid-market engagements land. Devin's framing: "It's mid-market scale where you've had to hire people, so you've had to write SOPs, so you've had to build systems." That history is what makes the contract viable; it is also what makes the budget viable.

This is the band where the [Workshop → Blueprint → Project → Partnership Engagement Ladder](/playbooks/workshop-blueprint-project-partnership-ladder) works best. The workshop costs ~$5k, the blueprint costs $15-35k, the project costs $50-500k, the partnership runs at $200k-$2M annually. The client can absorb the workshop without procurement friction, and each subsequent tier earns its way in through the prior tier's value.
