# AI Eats Labor Allocation

*SaaS captured a tiny slice of corporate budgets. AI captures a much larger slice. The unit economics are 50-100x different. This re-rates everything about the services-vs-product debate in AI.*

Every business that buys software allocates money to two large buckets: the software bucket and the labor bucket. The software bucket is small (typically 1-3% of revenue). The labor bucket is huge (typically 30-60% of revenue). SaaS spent twenty years optimizing how to capture share of the software bucket, and the unit economics of SaaS were defined by that bucket's size. AI systems are not really software in the traditional sense. They are labor replacement. The bucket they capture share from is the labor bucket, and the unit economics are correspondingly larger.

## The arithmetic

Devin Karns of Custom AI Studio works the example with a law firm:

- A law firm with $1M/month in revenue allocates roughly $2k/month to all its software combined.
- A 10-seat SaaS product at $10/seat/month captures $100/month of that $2k.
- The same firm allocates $300k+/month to labor (salaries, benefits, contractors).
- An AI system that replaces some of that labor's work captures share of the $300k, not the $2k.

The result is that an AI consulting firm serving a single law firm can plausibly earn $50k-$300k/month from that one client. The same law firm as a SaaS customer would generate $100/month. The ratio is roughly 500-3000x.

## Why the difference is structural, not contingent

The structural reason traditional SaaS has small unit economics is that the buyer is paying for code that already exists, scaled across many customers. The cost to serve the next customer is near zero. The price-per-customer has to be low because the alternative is the customer building their own version internally, and the customer's evaluation is "is this worth the $10/seat we're paying."

The structural reason AI consulting has large unit economics is that the buyer is paying for outcomes that previously required hiring people to produce. The cost to serve a customer is not near zero (every engagement requires real work), but the value being produced is the value of the labor being displaced. The customer's evaluation is "is this worth the $300k/year we would otherwise pay employees to produce."

SaaS competes against "build it internally with a developer." AI consulting competes against "hire more people." The two evaluations produce wildly different willingness-to-pay numbers.

## Where AI does compete with SaaS

The exception that proves the rule: most legacy SaaS categories have already been priced against the software allocation, which means an AI-native replacement that prices at the labor allocation will be wildly more expensive than the SaaS it displaces. The displacement still happens, but it happens with new contract structures that frame the value differently.

Example: A customer-support SaaS like Zendesk charges roughly $80/agent/month. A 50-agent firm pays Zendesk $4k/month. An AI-native customer-support system that replaces 80% of the agent work would, by the labor-allocation logic, command 80% of the 50-agent payroll, which might be $250k/month. The SaaS framing ($4k/month) and the labor-replacement framing ($250k/month) are not the same product even if the technical substrate is similar. The latter is sold as a labor-replacement system with attached service, not as software.

## The valuation implications

This is the single biggest reason AI consulting firms can re-rate at high multiples (see [The Services-Firm Re-Rating Threshold](/perspectives/services-firm-re-rating-threshold)). The traditional services-firm valuation logic prices the firm against its software-allocation peers and assumes services-firm margins. AI-consulting firms are operating in a different unit-economic regime: services-firm operating shape, but labor-replacement pricing power. Acquirers who understand this assign higher multiples accordingly.

It is also the reason the "AI agency" lifestyle businesses can grow much faster than 2020-era automation agencies. The pricing power is structurally higher per engagement, which means the same operator hours produce dramatically more revenue.

## The implications for pricing

Three operational shifts follow:

### 1. Price against the labor allocation, not the software allocation

The instinct of most AI consultants is to price like SaaS: per seat, per month, in the $10-100/seat range. The framing is wrong. AI systems should be priced against the labor they replace or augment. If the system replaces 2 FTEs at $80k/year fully-loaded, the system is worth a meaningful fraction of $160k/year, not $50/seat/month.

### 2. Use performance-based partnership structures

Time-and-materials pricing leaves the labor-replacement value on the table by definition. Performance-based pricing (see [The AI Tech Partnership Revenue Model](/playbooks/ai-tech-partnership-revenue-model)) captures it directly. The client pays a percentage of the labor savings or the revenue uplift, which puts the consultant on the right side of the value equation.

### 3. Frame the sale as a make-or-hire decision, not a buy-software decision

The buyer's mental model determines what they will pay. If they think they are buying software, they will pay software-bucket dollars. If they think they are making a make-or-hire decision (where the system is the make alternative to hiring more people), they will pay labor-bucket dollars. The framing happens on the first sales call.

## The historical precedent

This shift is not unprecedented. Cloud computing pulled some of the data-center allocation into the software allocation, which is how AWS and Azure became the largest software companies in history. Marketing automation pulled some of the marketing-services allocation into software. Each generation of technology that eats an adjacent budget category produces a wave of much larger software-shaped companies. AI is doing the same to labor.

The 2024-2026 wave of AI-native services companies (the YC-and-Sequoia "AI native services" thesis) is exactly this dynamic. Operators are realizing that the right shape is not "SaaS with AI features" but "services-priced-against-labor delivered by AI." The unit economics are categorically better.
