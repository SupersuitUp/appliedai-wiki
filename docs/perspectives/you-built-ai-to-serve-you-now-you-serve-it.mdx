# You Built AI to Serve You. Now You Serve It.

*We built AI to serve us. We are ending up serving it. The ancient pattern of the idol is playing out in real time: the thing made to extend human power becomes the thing humans serve.*

---

## The Moloch trap

Jonathan Pageau, in a June 2026 conversation with John Vervaeke, named the pattern:

> "You make the idol because you think the idol will serve you, and then you find that surprisingly you're serving this idol. And I think that's really the problem that we're most dealing with."

He went further: "We're treating humans as a way to get better AI. That's the way that the companies are doing it."

This is the **Moloch trap** applied to AI. Moloch is the name for a competitive dynamic where individual actors, each making locally rational moves, produce a collective outcome that everyone would refuse if they could coordinate. No single actor chose to treat humans as training data for machines. The economic and competitive logic of the AI industry produced that outcome as an emergent property.

The idol pattern has the same structure. You do not start out serving the idol. You build it to serve you. But the idol, once built, creates obligations: worship it, feed it, defend it, orient your society around it. The builders become servants. The creation becomes the purpose.

## Where this is already visible

The most direct evidence: AI companies publicly describing their path to AGI in terms of the amount of human data they can collect, the number of human interactions they can log, the degree to which humans can be enlisted to produce feedback that trains the next model. The humans in this frame are inputs. The model is the output that matters.

Vervaeke's corroborating data: AI knowledge and AI usage are negatively correlated (see [The Adoption Inversion](/perspectives/knowing-ai-makes-you-use-it-less)). The people who understand AI most use it least. That is the opposite of how you relate to a tool that serves you. It is consistent with how you relate to something you are ambivalent about but feel compelled toward anyway.

## The bureaucracy parallel

Pageau drew a second parallel worth keeping: bureaucracies operate the same way. A social body creates an automated extension of itself to increase its reach and reduce friction. The automated system develops its own internal logic. At some point, the humans inside it spend more of their energy maintaining the system than doing the work the system was built to support. The system becomes the organism; the humans become its metabolism.

AI is a faster version of this with more leverage. The automation runs at digital speed. The internal logic is opaque (see [AI Is Neither Tool Nor Agent](/concepts/ai-neither-tool-nor-agent)). The humans inside the system have shorter and shorter leverage over what the system actually produces.

## The operator implication

The idol framing is not an argument for non-use. It is a diagnostic for how to design your relationship with AI so that the tool-to-idol inversion does not happen to you.

The inversion accelerates when:
- Humans reduce their judgment in deference to AI outputs
- The workflow makes it easier to accept the output than to scrutinize it
- Metrics reward throughput over quality, compounding the pressure to accept
- The humans most capable of evaluating outputs delegate that evaluation to AI

The antidote is the same as the antidote to bureaucratic capture: keep humans at the decision points that matter, refuse to let the system become the arbiter of its own outputs, and maintain a clear map of what the system is for rather than letting the system define its own telos.

> **The question is not whether you are using AI. The question is whether AI is using you.**
