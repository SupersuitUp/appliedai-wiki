# The Services-Firm Re-Rating Threshold

*Services businesses sell at 1-2x EBITDA below roughly $5M annual revenue and 4-5x EBITDA above it. The threshold is the structural reason the exit posture decision matters.*

A services firm that crosses roughly $5M in annual revenue re-rates exponentially in the eyes of acquirers. Below the threshold, buyers see a founder-dependent practice and price it like one (1-2x EBITDA). Above the threshold, buyers see a portable operation with documented processes and a team that runs it (4-5x EBITDA). The number is fuzzy at the edges and varies by category, but the structural pattern holds across services categories including AI consulting, and it is the reason the [Lifestyle or Enterprise-Value Posture](/perspectives/lifestyle-or-enterprise-value-posture) decision matters as much as it does.

## The math

Devin Karns of Custom AI Studio articulates the math directly:

> "If you're making $2 million a year as a pure AI readiness consultant, you'll sell that business for probably $2 million. But if you're making $6 million a year, you can sell it for $30 million."

The same firm in three different revenue states:

| Annual revenue | EBITDA assumption | Multiple | Sale price |
|---|---|---|---|
| $2M | $1M (50% margin) | 1-2x | $1-2M |
| $4M | $2M | 1-2x | $2-4M |
| $6M | $2.4M (40% margin) | 4-5x | $10-12M (or $30M+ if 12x on premium categories) |
| $10M | $4M | 4-5x | $16-20M |

The exact multiples shift by quality and category. The structural shape does not. A $4M agency is worth roughly what a $2M agency is worth. A $6M agency is worth 5x what a $4M agency is worth. The biggest year of value creation in a services-firm builder's career is the year they cross the threshold.

## Why the re-rating happens

Acquirers below the threshold are buying a founder-shaped practice that will probably collapse if the founder leaves. They underwrite the cash flows for a few years, attach a discount for founder dependency, and arrive at a 1-2x EBITDA number. Above the threshold, three things have usually become true:

1. **The work is portable.** The firm has documented processes (see [Framework-First Positioning](/perspectives/framework-first-positioning)) that survive without the founder running every engagement.
2. **The team is layered.** There is a layer of senior people who own client relationships and can be retained through an earnout.
3. **The revenue is contracted.** A meaningful share of revenue is retainer-shaped or partnership-shaped rather than one-off projects (see [The AI Tech Partnership Revenue Model](/playbooks/ai-tech-partnership-revenue-model)).

Acquirers will pay much higher multiples for revenue that has those three properties because the risk profile of buying it is fundamentally different.

## The threshold for AI consulting specifically

The general services threshold sits around $5M ARR. For AI consulting in 2026 the threshold may sit slightly higher or lower depending on category:

- **Pure AI readiness consulting and training** re-rates around $3-5M. Margins are high, but the work is inherently founder-bottlenecked unless productized.
- **Full-stack AI consulting and build** (Custom AI Studio's category) re-rates around $5-7M. The build component requires a real engineering team, which lifts the threshold but also lifts the multiple ceiling.
- **Managed AI services** with recurring revenue can re-rate earlier (around $3-4M) because the revenue quality is structurally better.

## The strategic implication

If you are running an AI consulting firm and your goal is an exit, you have to build past the threshold. A firm that sits at $3-4M ARR for years is in the worst possible spot: too big to be a comfortable lifestyle business, too small to clear the re-rating. Either compress back to a $1-2M lifestyle practice with high margins, or commit to crossing the threshold. The middle is the trap.

The corollary: the years between $2M and $5M are the brutal ones. The founder is doing more work than they want to. The team is large enough to need management but not large enough to run independently. The financial reward looks small compared to the effort. This is precisely the stretch where founders quit the enterprise-value path and accept lifestyle outcomes, often without realizing they were within a year of the re-rating.

## How to know if you are approaching the threshold

Three signals:

1. **Revenue is over $3M and growing.** The threshold becomes a real planning question rather than a hypothetical.
2. **A meaningful share of revenue (>40%) is contracted or retainer-shaped.** The acquirer's underwriting math starts to work.
3. **The founder is the rate-limiter, not the talent floor.** When the founder is the only person who can close, sell, or own a client relationship, the firm is still a lifestyle business shaped like an enterprise one. The next hire is a senior client lead.
