# Humans As Instigators And Editors

*The human role in AI-native work splits into two anchors: instigators who kick off the work and define what success looks like, and editors who curate the output and decide what ships. The agent fills the middle.*

---

## The shift

Board of Innovation describes the role change as humans-as-creators becoming humans-as-editors. We extend the framing: editing alone undersells what the human does in an AI-native workflow. The human is also the instigator. The agent does not start. The agent does not decide whether to start. The agent does not define what counts as a finished version. Those acts sit upstream of any model call.

The clearer division of labor:

- **Instigator (human).** Notices the work needs doing. Sets the brief. Defines what success looks like. Holds the taste. Names the constraints. Decides when to start and when not to.
- **Drafter (agent).** Produces the first version. Iterates on instruction. Handles the volume work. Generates options.
- **Editor (human).** Reviews. Curates. Refines. Decides what ships. Maintains coherence across the body of work.

The agent fills the middle band. The two human anchors hold the work together.

## Why both ends matter

Frameworks that name only the editor anchor (humans-as-editors) imply that the agent already knows what to do and the human just polishes the output. In practice, that is not how the work goes. The hardest part of AI-augmented work is not the editing. It is the instigation: knowing what work to start, framing it precisely enough that the agent's draft is useful, and stopping the agent when the draft is going the wrong direction.

Frameworks that name only the instigator anchor (humans-as-prompters) imply that the agent's output is final, and that the only human craft is the prompt. In practice, agent drafts are almost never ready to ship without curation. The editing is where the work earns its quality.

Naming both ends produces a more accurate picture of the AI-augmented operator. It also produces a more useful job description: instigation and editing are the skills to develop in your team, not "prompt engineering" alone.

## What this implies for hiring and team design

The people who succeed in AI-augmented teams are those who can do both anchors well. That is not the same skill profile as the legacy "creator" role.

- **Instigation skills.** Strategic taste. Clarity about what counts as done. Comfort with ambiguity. The ability to write a brief that an agent can act on. Active curiosity about what work the team should be doing at all.
- **Editing skills.** Decisive judgment. Pattern recognition for slop. Willingness to discard ten drafts to keep the eleventh. The ability to maintain a coherent body of work across many agent outputs.

People who are strong at one anchor and weak at the other create a specific kind of failure mode. Strong-instigator-weak-editor teams ship a lot, with quality issues. Strong-editor-weak-instigator teams produce high-quality output of the wrong things. The teams that compound are strong at both.

## What this implies for tooling

The harness around the agent has to support both anchors:

- **For instigators:** good interfaces for setting briefs, defining success criteria, and steering early. Cheap experimentation. Fast feedback on whether the agent has understood the brief.
- **For editors:** good interfaces for comparing options, batch-reviewing drafts, and maintaining a quality bar across many outputs. Easy access to previous decisions for coherence.

Most current AI tooling is built for the drafter band (the agent does the work) and underinvests in the two human bands. The teams that will outperform are the ones that invest in the bands the tools are not optimizing.
