# Intelligence Is Commoditized, Deployment Is the Moat

*Every company can buy the same frontier intelligence, so intelligence is no longer the edge. The advantage is the bridge between general intelligence and one company's specific reality.*

## The intelligence is on tap, and everyone has the same tap

There was a theory that people would be priced out of intelligence, that the frontier would concentrate advantage in whoever could afford the best model. Maybe that arrives someday. It is not today. Today a frontier model ships what feels like every week, and every company can buy the same one. Walk into fifty enterprises and they run the same stack: the same coding agents, the same model-agnostic editors, the same copilots. If everyone can access the same foundational capability, that capability cannot be the moat.

This is the premise operators keep getting wrong. They treat access to a strong model as if it were the advantage. Access is the table stakes. The advantage is somewhere else.

## The advantage moved to deployment

If intelligence is general and available to all, the edge is no longer who has it. The edge is where, how, and why they apply it. Every company is different: different processes, different software, different exception handling, different things that actually matter to the business. General intelligence has to be applied to this specific company in the specific way that benefits it most. That application work is deployment, and it is where the value now concentrates.

Palantir proved this shape in the data age. Its platform was not remarkable for how tech-forward it was; it was remarkable for how customizable it was, and for the engineers who went on site to customize it per client until it solved that client's real pain better than any general product could. The AI age demands the same move at far greater scale, because every company now needs customized agents wired into its own systems.

## Why this reframes the whole game

Three consequences follow, and each one redirects where an operator should spend effort.

- **The scarce, expensive, defensible thing is the bridge, not the brain.** The person or firm that best connects a company's own processes to the intelligence stack wins. That is the reason the [Forward Deployed Engineer](/roles/forward-deployed-engineer) has become one of the best-paid seats in technology.
- **Model choice is downstream, not upstream.** Since the models are converging and all broadly available, marrying yourself to one provider is a liability, not an identity. Value lives in understanding both the business and the technology well enough to point any model at the right target. This is the same reason to [default to determinism](/perspectives/default-to-determinism) and route to a model only where judgment genuinely earns it.
- **The failures were deployment failures, not intelligence failures.** The era of "slap AI everywhere and let the model figure it out" produced hallucinations and the widely-cited statistic that 95% of generative AI pilots fail. The intelligence was fine. The deployment was absent. Handing a strong model to everyone with no bridge is how a C-suite burns a multi-million-dollar model budget in a quarter and does not move the business.

## The operator takeaway

Stop competing on which model you use, and stop treating access as an achievement. Compete on the quality of the bridge: how deeply you understand the real work, how well you decide where intelligence belongs, and how cleanly you deploy it into systems that already exist. The intelligence is a commodity input. The deployment is the product.
