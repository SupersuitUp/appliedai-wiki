# The Flaming Red Elephant in the Room

*The obvious political reality that AI discourse keeps refusing to name. Red for the revolutionary spirit rising in a generation locked out of the economy. Flaming for the firebombs already being thrown at AI executives and data centers. Elephant because the cause is obvious and nobody at the top of the industry wants to look at it.*

There is an obvious thing sitting in the middle of the AI conversation in 2026 that almost nobody with a podium wants to name.

A generation of young people has been told publicly, by the people building AI, that they are about to become economically "useless." That generation already cannot afford rent. They have watched every institution they were supposed to trust fail to steward society in their interest. Now the same tech elite is pouring hundreds of billions into infrastructure sited in communities those communities did not want, while selling investors on AI as the thing that will "take your job, be smarter than you, take your girlfriend, and ruin your life."

The result is exactly what a careful observer would predict. Booed commencement speakers. Firebombs at executives' homes. Shot-up councilmen's houses. Comment sections cheering it on. A growing share of young people convinced that stopping AI by any means necessary is a heroic calling.

Call this the **Flaming Red Elephant in the Room**. Three parts, each one obvious once stated, each one actively ignored by AI industry leadership and AI doom leadership both.

---

## Red

Red is the color of the revolutionary spirit rising in a generation that feels abandoned by every existing institution, and the rise is understandable.

When the people at the top of the wealth distribution publicly describe you as "useless," and the companies building the biggest new technology in the world market it as the thing that will ruin your life, the red rising in response is a predictable historical pattern in every similar moment. Dismissing it as fringe, conspiracy, or adolescent is what the people who caused it would like everyone else to do. It is also what guarantees the spiral accelerates.

The clearest fresh evidence landed on May 18, 2026. An ex-Google CEO took the commencement podium at the University of Arizona, started talking about AI, and got booed by the graduating class. The clip went up on WSJ News the same day: [Ex-Google CEO Gets Booed While Discussing AI in Commencement Speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tNH43a1EI7s). The top comments under the video are the artifact worth studying:

> "I understand that fear" bro you're a billionaire, you don't feel a lick of it.

> Don't ask what AI can do for you, Ask what you can do for AI.

> If "out of touch with reality" was a person.

> He thought he was speaking at Stanford.

> The kids are alright.

That is the temperature of the room. A man at the apex of the industry, addressing 22-year-olds about to enter the worst entry-level job market in modern memory, telling them to feel better about AI. The room booed him in real time, and the internet sided with the room.

The inverse reading on the same room landed in the same week. A small-town farmer's on-camera refusal of a $26 million offer from a data center developer ([BowmanTV, May 2026](https://youtu.be/Wlw9m4ADNDA)) is being canonized in the comments under the clip as folk heroism. The top replies read "This woman is a national treasure," "Much respect to NOT sell out," "This woman saved her town," "A person of integrity. A rare quality nowadays," and "Shes not ruled by greed." The room that booed the billionaire ex-CEO in Tucson is the same room making a hero of the woman who said no to the check. Both readings are the same reading.

The political energy of 2026 is materially more desperate and ideologically more radical than the energy of any decade in recent memory, and the radicalism is showing up on both ends of the horseshoe. Underestimating that is how institutions fail in the years ahead. See [The Elevator Economy](/perspectives/the-elevator-economy) for the foundational economic observation underneath it.

## Flaming

Flaming is the literal fire. It is not rhetoric. It is the Molotov cocktails thrown twice in two weeks at Sam Altman's house. It is the thirteen rounds fired into an Indianapolis city councilman's home over a data center vote with an eight-year-old asleep inside. It is the cheering in the comments under every news article covering those events. It is what is next.

[Dylan Patel](https://x.com/dylan522p), founder of SemiAnalysis, on Patrick O'Shaughnessy's podcast ([clip](https://x.com/patrick_oshag/status/2047390954382893514)) named the trajectory directly:

> I think there will be a large-scale protest against Anthropic. End up at AI. People hate AI. AI is less popular than ICE, less popular than politicians. With Anthropic adding so much revenue, that's going to start causing business changes downstream. People are going to get more and more scared of AI. They'll start blaming more and more of their own problems and things that have been deep-seated problems for a long time. Those will bubble up and be blamed on AI. Probably some politician or influencer will be able to start taking and weaponizing AI against people. You look at the comments of news articles where Sam Altman had a Molotov cocktail thrown in his house twice in like two weeks. They're like, people are cheering it on. And this is just the beginning. So I think we'll see large-scale protests against AI in three months.

Patel's advice to the AI industry in the same interview is worth quoting alongside it:

> First of all, Sam Altman and Dario have to stop getting on interviews. They're so uncharismatic. Every interview they do is like, wow, normal people are going to hate you even more. Sam being on Tucker Carlson probably made all Republicans hate OpenAI. And same with Dario. They just have no charisma. That's first. Two, they need to start showing uplifting things that can be done with AI. Three, they need to stop talking about how the capabilities are going to change the whole world constantly, because then people are going to get fear of that capability, because they have no connection. The average person doesn't know an Anthropic employee. The average person doesn't know an OpenAI employee. They just view them as this sneaky cabal of 5,000 people at this company that are going to change the world and automate all the jobs and destroy society, and as people who are funding the building of all these data centers and power plants that are going to pollute the world. I think it's a huge reorg and rebranding that needs to be done.

The escalation curve everyone in physical security is now pricing in goes by one word. Drones. And the worry inside the industry is no longer about a single class of attacker. The list of people interested in crippling the American AI elite now spans foreign nation-states, domestic anti-AI activists, lone opportunists, displaced workers, local communities resisting data-center construction, and ideologically motivated cells that overlap with none of the above. A consumer drone does not care which of those rooms its operator came from. The principals funding the buildout have started saying the same thing in public, in their own words, on tape.

The nation-state version of the warning has already cashed. On March 1, 2026, Iranian Shahed drones struck two Amazon Web Services data centers in the United Arab Emirates, with a third commercial site hit in Bahrain two weeks later. It was the first time a nation-state had directly targeted commercial data centers in wartime. By early April, Iranian state media had named the rest of the list: Microsoft, Google, Apple, Meta, Oracle, Intel, HP, IBM, Cisco, Dell, Palantir, Nvidia. Oracle's Dubai data center was hit on April 2. AWS chief Matt Garman told CNBC his teams were [working around the clock](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/07/aws-iran-threats-us-tech-data-centers.html) to keep regional services up. The question that surfaced in the trade press the same week is the one [Axios put on the page](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/01/iran-war-data-center-gulf-ai) directly: should commercial data centers now be defended like military assets?

The inside-the-house version of the same warning landed two months later, from one of the principals funding the buildout. On The Tucker Carlson Show on May 14, 2026, in a [widely-discussed exchange about the proposed Utah Stratopolis data center](https://www.sltrib.com/news/2026/05/15/tucker-carlson-kevin-oleary-debate/), BlackRock CEO Larry Fink raised the specific scenario of "domestic terrorism" using a "$3,000 drone" against the AI infrastructure his firm is positioned to control. The threat actor named in that warning is not a foreign military. It is one ordinary American with a grievance and a consumer credit card. The pre-emptive framing of that citizen as a domestic terrorist is a separate thing the field will have to reckon with. The salient point is the dollar amount. The person at the top of the asset stack now publicly believes the floor on attacking his infrastructure is three thousand dollars.

The strikes and the warning describe the same math. A consumer-grade FPV drone costs under $500 and an off-the-shelf commercial system runs in the low thousands. The targets either one can reach are worth ten billion dollars and look like aircraft hangars from above. The asymmetric warfare logic that has been running for three years in Ukraine has now been demonstrated in a live conflict between a nation-state and a commercial cloud provider, and the same logic has been named on cable television by an American billionaire as his domestic risk. The domestic actors who already cheered Molotov cocktails at Sam Altman's house in April are watching that math the same way the industry is.

The structural parallel writes itself. The American AI buildout is a fleet of Death Stars. Each data center is a multi-hundred-acre, multi-billion-dollar concentration of capability that justifies its cost only at superweapon scale, and each one ships with the same kind of vulnerability the original had: a single physical entry point that turns its size from an asset into a liability. The Yavin exhaust port was the size of a womp rat. This generation's equivalents are the cooling intake, the water main, the perimeter HVAC fan, and the unguarded substation feeding the campus. The comment thread under [The Jimmy Dore Show's May 19, 2026 clip of the Fink remark](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLvIlTaJPPs) is worth reading literally. The top-rated reply, posted within hours of upload and sitting at 1.1K likes, names the next exhaust port without flinching:

> Take out their cooling systems and they'll fry on their own.

The replies underneath argue over methods.

> Cut off the water supply upstream / up pipe!

> Absolutely. No water, No centres. No Brainer.

> they will run the pipe systems way underground into the ground water system suck it from the source. But there are still ways.

That the public conversation has already moved past drones to the next exhaust port is the data point worth holding. The drone was the first vector the industry priced. It will not be the last.

The hardening response is moving fast and showing up in the prices. The global drone-defense market is [sized at $8.8 billion in 2026 and projected to reach $146 billion by 2035](https://www.marketgrowthreports.com/blog/drone-defense-system-186) at a 36.7% compound rate. Robb Report's [coverage of high-end private bunkers in 2026](https://robbreport.com/shelter/celebrity-homes/wealthy-homeowners-building-luxury-survivalist-bunkers-1236905429/) lists "drone-defense hatches" alongside panic rooms and gun ranges in the standard ultra-luxury build-out. The Department of Defense announced its [largest-ever investment in drones and anti-drone weapons](https://defensescoop.com/2026/04/21/dod-plans-largest-ever-investment-drones-anti-drone-weapons/) in April. On May 19, 2026, the same week a graduating class booed an ex-Google CEO off a commencement stage in Tucson, the President announced [a new bunker-like ballroom at the White House with a drone base on the roof](https://www.militarytimes.com/news/pentagon-congress/2026/05/19/trump-reveals-new-details-of-bunker-like-ballroom-with-drone-base/). The people closest to the AI buildout are pricing the next escalation as a physical-infrastructure question, and the bill is being itemized in public.

The fire is no longer hypothetical. The question every AI institution has to answer in 2026 is what it is actually doing about the conditions underneath the fire, and whether its public posture is pouring water on the flames or kerosene.

## Elephant

Elephant is the fact that the cause is obvious and the industry will not say it out loud.

The polite version of AI discourse treats the field as a meritocratic collaboration of curious researchers. The actual top of the industry is steered in meaningful part by a small class of transhumanist billionaires whose public statements have equivocated on whether ordinary human flourishing is even the point of AI. Several are personally entangled in the Epstein scandal. Some openly hold a "religion of intelligence" worldview in which AGI is a successor species rather than a tool built for human service. None of this is hidden or obscure. It is on the record in their own books, interviews, and open letters.

The AI doom movement on the other side of the conversation is funded by money that overlaps with these same networks. The two loudest sides of the AI debate trace back to a remarkably small set of people whose incentives diverge sharply from the public's. One side tells ordinary people they will be economically useless. The other side tells them the whole project is extinction-level evil. Both sides are currently speaking past the actual twenty-year-old trying to afford rent.

That is the elephant. The field cannot have an honest conversation about applied AI's promise for humanity while pretending the top of the industry is stewarded by people who unambiguously want that outcome. Naming the elephant is the first honest move available. Closing the exclusion is how the elephant stops being on fire.

## What this means for the practitioner

Every applied AI engineer who runs a workshop, ships a product, publishes a case study, or has a conversation about AI in public is operating inside the temperature this concept describes. The landscape is not neutral, and the assumption of neutrality is a posture that only reads as safe to people already inside the gates.

The implication is not to pick a side in the discourse. The implication is that every AI initiative now requires real delicacy and self-awareness about the room it is landing in. The room is hot. Default industry posture is making it hotter. Nobody shipping AI work in 2026 gets to opt out of that backdrop.

Some industry voices are starting to name what the alternative posture looks like. Will Manidis, in [an April 2026 X thread](https://x.com/WillManidis/status/2047349458132717933), called out Meta specifically:

> its impossible to overstate how well facebook is doing on comms surrounding data centers and models right now. offer jobs and training, clearly state the truth over water and power usage, don't do doomer fiction. just a relentless execution of the basics. no one else is even close.

And in a follow-up:

> not getting your data centers blown up is the only factor input to scaling laws that still makes this game a toss up.

That second line is the elephant named from inside the industry. The physical security of the AI buildout is now a scaling-laws-level input, sitting next to compute, data, and algorithms on the list of things that determine whether the whole project works. The inputs to that security are not technical. They are political and relational: jobs in the communities hosting the infrastructure, honest public communication about water and power, the absence of doomer framing, and a visible path for ordinary people into the economy the buildout is enabling.

For an applied AI practitioner, that translates into a handful of operating habits:

- **Speak about AI as if the room is in the room.** Most audiences in 2026 include people who fear what is happening more than they understand it. Posture, word choice, and what gets emphasized all read as signals about whose side the speaker is on.
- **Land the technology on the person in front of you.** A workshop that leaves an ordinary participant operating a working system is a different artifact than a keynote about how powerful the technology has become. One is reassuring. The other is alarming.
- **Watch the words the public has learned to hate.** "Useless class," "automate everything," "replace the workforce," "AGI is coming for your job." These are no longer neutral technical claims. They are how the field has been losing the public.
- **Acknowledge the political reality when it is operative.** When a client, a community group, or an audience is acting out of a fear connected to the elephant, treating the fear as irrational is the wrong move. The fear has a cause. Acknowledge it and proceed.
- **Build with communities, not on them.** Where AI infrastructure or AI products land in a real place with real people, a practitioner who shows up only after the deployment is part of the problem the elephant describes.

Naming the elephant is not activism. It is professional self-awareness. The applied AI engineer who pretends the political temperature does not exist is shipping into a context they refuse to see, and shipping that way produces the exact outcome the field most wants to avoid.
