# Build, Don't Buy Your Way In

*Buying a legacy services firm and bolting AI on top is a trap: you cannot acquire product-market fit, and legacy expectations on metrics, hiring, and performance do not change because you added AI. The one exception is a regulatory moat you need fast.*

---

There is a tempting shortcut into the [AI-native services](/concepts/ai-native-services-company) opportunity, and it is most tempting to founders with an operating background: buy an existing services business, add some AI on top, and short-circuit the years it takes to build revenue from scratch. You start with customers, cash flow, and a team. It looks like a head start. It is generally a trap.

## Why it almost never works

You cannot acquire product-market fit. The thing that makes an AI-native services company work is a delivery operation designed from the ground up around models doing most of the work, and that is precisely what a legacy firm does not have. Legacy service businesses are, definitionally, legacy: they carry different expectations on metrics, hiring, and performance, built over years around human-powered delivery. Adding AI on top of that does not change any of those realities. You inherit an organization optimized for the old cost structure and the old margins, and the AI sits on the surface of a body that was never shaped to use it.

The result is not an AI-native company with a revenue head start. It is a legacy company with an AI feature and a founder now spending all their leverage fighting the installed culture instead of building the machine. The [operating leverage](/concepts/ai-operating-leverage) that justifies the whole model never materializes, because the operation underneath was built for a different bet.

## The one decent reason to buy

There is a single exception worth taking seriously: when you need a regulatory moat fast. Insurance licensing is the canonical case. If the only way into the market is a license that takes years to obtain, buying an entity that already holds it can be the rational move, because the asset you are acquiring is the regulatory standing, not the operation. In that narrow case you are buying a key, not a company, and you still build the operation fresh behind the door it opens.

Outside of that, building is almost always better than buying.
