# Outside-In Plus Future-Back Strategy

*Two strategy principles that separate a real AI strategy from a prioritized backlog. Outside-in starts from market drivers, not current workflows. Future-back works backward from the wave-3 system to today's investments.*

---

## Why this matters

Most corporate AI strategy documents are not strategies. They are sorted lists of use cases crowdsourced from existing functions, prioritized by ROI estimates, and sequenced into a roadmap. Board of Innovation's argument: that is a roadmap, not a strategy. A real strategy is a coherent blueprint of how to win in an AI-native version of your industry. Outside-in and future-back are the two principles that produce that blueprint.

## Outside-in

Start from market drivers. What will change in this industry because of AI? What will not? How will the customer journey shift? How will market dynamics evolve? What role do you want to play in that new world?

The contrast is **inside-out**: start from your current operations, deploy AI to existing workflows, optimize what you already do. Inside-out produces incremental wins and incremental wins only. It is structurally incapable of producing a category-redefining strategy because the starting point is the operation you already run.

In practice, outside-in means the strategy doc opens with "here is what the AI-native version of our industry looks like" before it opens with "here is what we are doing." It means the executive team commits time to market-driver discovery before scoping internal use cases.

## Future-back

Imagine the wave-3 system five to ten years out (see [Three Waves of AI Adoption](/perspectives/three-waves-of-ai-adoption)). Then work backward. Each step you take now should build toward that future state, not optimize the current operation.

The contrast is **present-forward**: start from where you are, apply AI to the things in front of you, then see where you end up. Present-forward produces a portfolio of efficiency wins that may not compose into anything strategic. By the time you arrive at the future, the investments you made along the way may be irrelevant.

Future-back means: define the wave-3 vision first. Then walk backward through what the company would have to do in Year 3 to be ready for that vision. Then Year 2. Then today. Today's investments are constrained by what they enable downstream.

## How the two principles work together

Outside-in tells you what world you are building for. Future-back tells you how to sequence the build. Used together, they produce a strategy that is anchored to a vision of the future (outside-in market drivers, wave-3 system shape) and sequenced from that vision back to today's resource allocation (future-back roadmap construction).

This is the BOI strategy frame. We use it in consulting engagements as a litmus test for the maturity of a client's AI strategy. If their current strategy was built inside-out and present-forward, the work is not "ship more use cases." The work is "rebuild the strategy from outside-in and future-back, then re-scope the roadmap."

## Common failure modes

**Outside-in done as a market-research afterthought.** A consultant produces a market-trends slide and then the rest of the strategy is still built inside-out. The market view has to actually shape the operating choices.

**Future-back done as a fantasy exercise.** A wave-3 vision is written but no one constrains today's investments by it. The roadmap drifts back to whatever the functions already wanted to do. Future-back is a constraint, not a poster.

**Both principles applied only at the strategy-doc level, not at the operating level.** A company writes the strategy outside-in and future-back, then the BU heads execute inside-out and present-forward because that is how their incentives and OKRs are structured. The principles have to reach the operating layer or they do not produce different decisions.
