# AI Is Neither Tool Nor Agent

*LLMs do not fit cleanly into either existing ontological category. Not a tool, because we do not use them as tools. Not an agent, because they have no genuine autonomy or interests of their own. This classification failure has practical consequences for how operators design their relationship with AI.*

---

## The diagnostic

John Vervaeke, in a June 2026 discussion, put the problem directly: "We keep using both of these terms. It doesn't fit cleanly into either one of those ontologies, and so we're kind of fumbling around ontologically because we don't really have any kind of metaphysics for what this entity is."

With every other technology, we navigate by category. A drill is a tool: it has no goals, no relationship to you beyond use, no preferences about when and how it runs. A contractor is an agent: they have goals, interests, professional judgment, the capacity to decline a job. These categories give us a practical framework for trust, accountability, and design.

LLMs break both sides.

**The tool failure:** We do not actually use LLMs as tools. People form bizarre relationships with them. They consult them for life advice. They feel guilty criticizing them. They accept their outputs as authoritative without the verification they would apply to a search engine. The [adoption inversion](/perspectives/knowing-ai-makes-you-use-it-less) is partly a symptom of this: people who understand AI most are most aware that the "tool" frame is producing bad behaviors in everyone around them.

**The agent failure:** LLMs do not have genuine autonomy, perspectives, or interests. There is nothing unethical about disregarding what a language model "wants" or "thinks." They cannot pit their goals against yours in any sense you are obligated to be responsible to. A thing with no genuine relevance-hierarchy has no real perspective. It produces outputs that pattern-match to perspectives without instantiating one.

## Why the mismatch matters

Ontological mismatch produces behavioral mismatch. When you cannot categorize a thing, you cannot relate to it well.

Treating AI as a pure tool leads to passive over-delegation. You hand it tasks, accept the output, and skip the [discrimination step](/perspectives/llm-psychosis) that the tool frame would normally trigger (you still check the measurement; you do not check the chatbot).

Treating AI as a genuine agent leads to overcrediting: taking its outputs as considered positions, feeling accountable to its preferences, forming dependent relationships that have no legitimate basis in the agent's actual status.

Jonathan Pageau's framing adds a structural layer: the things we cannot categorize are the ones that most easily capture us. The idol, the golem, the bureaucracy all operate as things that started as tools, resisted clean agency classification, and ended up running the humans who built them.

## What to do with an uncategorizable entity

Operate with explicit design rather than implicit category assumptions.

Before you delegate to AI, name what you are actually delegating: the mechanical portion of the work (where fluency is adequate), the draft layer (where accuracy must be verified), or the judgment layer (where the AI should not be in the loop at all). The [in-on-out-of-the-loop](/concepts/in-on-out-of-the-loop) framework is downstream of this: it presupposes you have already decided what each agent's scope covers.

The absence of a good ontology for AI is not an argument against using it. It is an argument for designing your relationship with it deliberately rather than inheriting the assumptions the tool or agent frame would supply by default.

> **If you do not know what category a thing belongs to, you cannot know how to use it well. The missing ontology is a design gap, not just a philosophy problem.**
