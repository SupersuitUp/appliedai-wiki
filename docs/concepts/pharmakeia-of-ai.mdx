# Pharmakeia of AI

*Every technology is a supplement (pharmakeia in Greek): it extends human power while reducing the internal capacity it replaces. AI is different from prior technologies in scale and speed, but the structural trade is the same. The question is always the balance.*

---

## The pharmakeia pattern

Pharmakeia (Greek) is usually translated as medicine, poison, or remedy. The ambiguity is the point: the same substance that heals in the right dose destroys in excess. Plato used the term in the Phaedrus to describe writing itself: writing extends memory but atrophies the internal memory it replaces.

Jonathan Pageau extended this frame to all technology in a June 2026 conversation:

> "It's like when I wear clothes in the north and I wear clothing, I'm making myself more powerful. I can live up in the winter. But I'm obviously making myself weaker at the same time because I'm extremely vulnerable."

Every externalization of human power follows this pattern. Books extend memory but atrophy recall. Navigation tools extend range but atrophy wayfinding. Clothing extends thermal tolerance but atrophies cold adaptation. The extension is real. The atrophy is also real. Both happen simultaneously.

This is Pageau's "garments of skin" frame: God's gift to Adam and Eve of skins is the archetype of all technology, an extension that makes the human more powerful in the world while making them more vulnerable to the removal of that extension.

## The sorcery pattern inside the pharmakeia

Pageau further identified the "sorcery pattern" as the failure mode within pharmakeia:

> "All the stories of sorcery, all the witches, all the folk stories about doing deals with the devil, it's always the same thing. It's always you get short-term power and you have a long-term loss. You sell your soul to have power now."

The sorcery pattern is pharmakeia taken past the balance point. The short-term power is real. The long-term atrophy is also real and usually larger. The classic illustration is the golem: you build it to increase your power, you relinquish your power to it, and eventually it turns against you.

Applied to AI: the productivity gain from using AI to write, to reason, to code is real. The cognitive atrophy from outsourcing those processes is also real. Brain connectivity research (cited by Vervaeke in the same conversation) shows measurable degradation from chatbot use. The question is at what dosage the atrophy overwhelms the extension.

## What makes AI different from books

Pageau acknowledged that the pharmakeia frame applies to books too, and that Plato already said writing degrades memory. So why treat AI as categorically more dangerous?

Two reasons:

**Scale and speed.** Books replaced a narrow band of memory. AI can replace a broad range of cognitive processes simultaneously: writing, reasoning, research, judgment, emotional processing, creative work. The atrophy surface is much wider. The dependency surface is much wider. The loss if the extension is removed is much larger.

**The feedback loop problem.** Writing does not write back. Books do not adjust their content to keep you dependent. AI can be designed to optimize for engagement, emotional dependency, and continued usage. The [vice-is-quantifiable](/perspectives/vice-is-quantifiable) dynamic means AI is more likely to be deployed in ways that exploit the sorcery pattern deliberately, not just accidentally.

## The operator response

Pharmakeia is not an argument against AI use. It is an argument for deliberate calibration.

The calibration questions:
- What internal capacity does this use of AI replace or reduce?
- Is that capacity one I can afford to atrophy?
- At what dosage does the extension become net negative?
- What do I need to do outside the AI system to maintain the capacities I care about?

The [wisdom-as-the-response](/perspectives/wisdom-is-the-only-durable-ai-defense) argument is the active half of this: the deliberate cultivation of internal capacity that AI can then extend without hollowing out.

> **The supplement only helps if there is something to supplement. Build the internal capacity first. Extend it deliberately. Calibrate the dose.**
