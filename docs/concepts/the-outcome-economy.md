# The Outcome Economy

*An economy where the sellable unit is an outcome generator: a system that embeds superintelligence to reliably produce a valued outcome on demand. You buy or integrate a generator, not a tool and not labor.*

Also called the **outcome generation economy**. The name marks a shift in what changes hands. In the tool economy you sold software and the buyer supplied the labor to operate it. In the labor economy you sold hours and the buyer supplied the direction. In the outcome economy you sell a machine that produces the finished result, and the buyer supplies neither the tool-operation nor the labor.

## The generator is the product, the outcome is its yield

The demand-side version of this idea is old and settled: people want outcomes, not tools. Nobody wants a drill, they want the hole. What has changed is the supply side. It is now possible to build and sell a system that produces the hole on demand, over and over, without a human turning the drill each time. That system is the **outcome generator**, and it is the thing you are selling. The outcome is what it yields.

Say plainly what is new, because "outcome economy" on its own is not new. Agencies have always sold outcomes: a law firm sells a closed deal, a marketing shop sells a launched campaign. The novel thing is selling a generator that produces the outcome on demand. The agency was itself a generator made of people, priced by the hour or the retainer and capped by how many people it could hire. An outcome generator built on superintelligence is not capped that way. You ship the generator once and it produces the outcome a thousand times, and the buyer integrates it into their business the way they integrate any other piece of infrastructure. This is the supply-side twin of [Convert Business Logic to AI System](/concepts/convert-business-logic-to-ai-system): the business logic, once encoded, becomes a generator someone will pay to run.

## Pricing: you do not sell tokens, you sell outcomes whose cost floor is tokens

Get the pricing story exactly right, because it is easy to say wrong. You do not sell tokens. Nobody wants tokens, the same way nobody wants a drill. Never tell a buyer "I sell tokens." What you sell is an outcome, and the honest thing to know about that outcome is that its variable cost floor is superintelligence, which is to say [tokens](/perspectives/the-token-economy).

Naming the cost floor is what makes value-proportional pricing defensible. Because the marginal cost of one more outcome is close to the token cost of producing it, price can scale with outcomes delivered rather than sitting as a flat seat license. The buyer pays in proportion to what they receive, which stays fair as usage grows: a buyer who generates ten outcomes pays roughly ten times what a buyer who generates one does, and the gap between price and cost stays honest at every volume. This is the pricing logic behind [Gain-Sharing](/concepts/gain-sharing), where the seller is paid a share of the value the generator creates rather than a fixed fee detached from results. The seat-license model breaks here, because a seat prices access to a tool and the outcome economy is not selling access to a tool.

## Reliability, not exoticism, is the defining trait

Do not over-read "embeds superintelligence" as if every generator has to be exotic or frontier. The defining trait of an outcome generator is that it reliably produces the outcome, on demand, at a quality the buyer will accept every time. Sometimes reliability comes from streamlining an existing process until the last human step is gone. Sometimes it comes from throwing the process out and reinventing how the outcome gets made. Both count. A generator that produces a mediocre outcome reliably can beat a generator that produces a brilliant outcome one time in three, because the buyer is integrating it and needs it to hold.

This is why building a generator often means letting go of the old process. If you assume the outcome must still be produced the way it always was, minus a few human steps, you cap the generator at the ceiling of the old process. The reinvention move, and the faith it takes to make it, is [Don't Get Attached to Your Process](/perspectives/dont-get-attached-to-your-process). The [product is an operation](/concepts/the-product-is-an-operation): what you hand the buyer is a running system that keeps producing, not a static artifact that sits still.

## More and more work becomes buying or integrating generators

Follow the shift forward and the shape of work changes. If the sellable unit is a generator, then a growing share of business activity is acquiring the right generators and wiring them into an operation. A company that wants closed books buys or integrates a books-closing generator. A company that wants a month of on-brand content integrates a content generator and points it at their brand. The buyer's job moves from doing the work, and from managing the people who do the work, toward selecting, integrating, and holding accountable the generators that do the work. Even selling into this economy takes the shape of a generator: [The Generative Welcome](/concepts/generative-welcome) hands a prospect a paste-in recipe that produces a personalized artifact on the spot, letting them experience the outcome before they buy the generator that makes it. Human judgment concentrates at the edges of that operation, where the generators meet reality and each other ([Humans at the Edges](/perspectives/humans-at-the-edges)).

The competitive claim underneath all of this: owning a good generator is a durable position, because [intelligence is commoditized and deployment is the moat](/perspectives/intelligence-is-commoditized-deployment-is-the-moat). Anyone can rent the superintelligence. The generator that reliably turns it into a specific valued outcome, integrated into a real operation, is the thing worth paying for.
