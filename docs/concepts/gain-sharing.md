## What it is

Gain sharing ties your fee to the gain. You agree on a number the work moves (dollars saved, revenue added, hours converted to output) and you take an agreed percentage of that number. Hourly bills your time. A retainer bills your availability. Gain sharing bills the result.

It sits one rung above a high retainer on the leverage ladder. A retainer caps your upside at the rate you negotiated. Gain sharing uncaps it: when the work creates a large, measurable gain, your fee rises with it. That is why it is the model for a consultancy that already knows it is good. If you are confident and capable enough to predict the gain you will create, you can often earn more than any retainer would have paid.

## The two shapes

Almost every gain-sharing deal is one of two shapes, or a blend of both.

- **Shared savings.** You take a percentage of the cost you remove. You find $400,000 a year of waste (manual work, tool sprawl, error rework) and keep an agreed cut of it. Common in cost-reduction work.
- **Upside share.** You take a percentage of the new value you create. You rebuild the sales team's workflow, bookings rise by $1M a year, and you keep an agreed cut of the lift. Common in revenue and productivity work.

Any function that maps cleanly to revenue or cost works the same way. The model does not care which lever you pulled. It cares that the lever moved a number both sides can read.

## When it beats a retainer

Gain sharing is a lever for the high-confidence, high-capability end of the market. Reach for it when:

- You have done the math and believe the gain will exceed what a retainer would pay.
- The gain is large enough that a percentage of it is a serious number.
- You have a track record that makes your prediction credible to the buyer.

For a capable consultancy this is the difference between selling your time and selling the outcome your time produces. The first has a ceiling. The second does not.

## What has to be true first

Gain sharing only works when the gain is measurable, attributable, and honored. Before you propose it, confirm three things.

- **A baseline exists.** You cannot share a gain you cannot measure against a clear "before."
- **The gain is attributable to you.** If ten other things changed at the same time, the client will not credit the lift to your work, and they will be right to hesitate.
- **The client will honor the count.** Agree up front on how the number is measured, who measures it, and when you get paid. A fuzzy definition becomes a fight at payout.

When any of these is shaky, anchor on a [retainer or hybrid](/playbooks/pricing) and keep gain sharing as the upside layer on top.

## What this is not

Gain sharing is not equity or revenue share. Equity bets on the whole company's future. Gain sharing is paid on a specific, bounded result you can point to and measure. You can stack it on a base fee, and a confident consultancy usually does: a smaller retainer to cover the work, plus a share of the gain to capture what the work is actually worth.
