# Picking an AI-Services Market

*A four-trait filter for choosing the market to build an AI-native services company in: low trust, low judgment at the task level, a high intelligence threshold, and regulation as a moat.*

---

For an [AI-native services company](/concepts/ai-native-services-company), market selection is the highest-leverage decision you make, because the wrong market makes the operation impossible to scale no matter how good the product is. Standard startup advice still applies (pick something you will love for a decade, because these companies take ten-plus years), but the AI-services lens adds a specific four-trait filter.

## The four traits

**1. Low trust.** The work is already outsourced, and the customer cares about the final product, not how it got made. You are displacing a vendor, not asking the customer to do something new. This matters enormously: you are not changing behavior, you are showing up where the budget already lives and doing the work. Markets where the customer insists on owning the process internally are a much harder sell.

**2. Low judgment at the task level.** Break the work into pieces. If every piece needs a human exercising real judgment, you cannot scale. You need most steps automatable, with judgment concentrated in a few places where humans stay in the loop. This is the same architecture as [the judgment line](/perspectives/the-judgment-line), applied to a whole market instead of a single system: the LLM and code carry the volume, humans carry the few decisions that genuinely need them.

**3. A high intelligence threshold.** This sounds like it contradicts trait two, but it does not. The overall work has to be genuinely hard, hard enough that models plus humans are required to deliver an outcome the customer accepts. Easy work gets commoditized by anyone, including the customer. The sweet spot is work that decomposes into mostly-automatable steps but is hard in aggregate.

**4. Regulation can be a moat.** Regulated industries carry higher expectations and legal accountability, which raises the bar and the moat for whoever clears it. The compliance gravity that scares off generalist competitors is exactly what protects you once you are inside. YC's Panacea is the example: FDA regulatory services for biotech and medtech, pairing experienced FDA consultants with an AI platform to deliver faster, higher-quality approvals.

## Known good-fit markets

Per YC: tax, audit, insurance, mortgages, parts of healthcare, parts of logistics. But plenty of untouched markets exist. Do not limit yourself to the obvious ones or to what people talk about on X.

## Two more checks

**The Sam Altman test.** As the models get better, does your service get stronger or does the model commoditize you? Build only where capability jumps are tailwinds. See [The Sam Altman Test](/concepts/the-sam-altman-test).

**Avoid physical operations.** Steer clear of anything involving equipment and on-site labor. The software-margin math does not apply when you own and operate physical things, so leverage is hard to create. These can be good businesses; they are just not this one. Leave them to the robotics founders.

**The honesty check.** Ask sincerely: are you using humans because the work genuinely needs judgment, or because you are compensating for product gaps? Be honest, so you are not papering over product shortcomings with headcount. This is the most portable line in the whole filter, and it applies long after market selection.
