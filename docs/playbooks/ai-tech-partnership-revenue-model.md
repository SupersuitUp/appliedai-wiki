The dominant pricing model in AI consulting is time-and-materials or fixed-scope project pricing. Both leave most of the value on the table. When an AI system is doing the work of ten employees at a fraction of the cost, charging $200/hour for the build wildly underprices the deliverable. The growth-marketing world solved this problem fifteen years ago by structuring partnerships around budget plus a percentage of attributable revenue. AI consulting is starting to adopt the same shape, and Custom AI Studio is among the firms making it the default for step 4 of their [Workshop → Blueprint → Project → Partnership Engagement Ladder](/playbooks/workshop-blueprint-project-partnership-ladder).

## What it produces

- Recurring annual contract values in the $200k-$2M+ range.
- Revenue that scales with the client's growth, not with the consultant's hours.
- Strategic alignment between consultant and client (when the client wins, the consultant wins).
- The kind of contracted, performance-linked revenue that re-rates the firm's valuation at exit (see [The Services-Firm Re-Rating Threshold](/perspectives/services-firm-re-rating-threshold)).
- Defensibility: a partnership-shaped engagement is structurally harder for a competitor to displace than a fixed-scope project.

## When it works

Three preconditions, all required:

1. **The AI system has a clear, attributable through-line to the P&L.** Refund rate, sales-cycle length, support-ticket-time, conversion rate, hiring funnel speed. Something where the consultant's system measurably moves a number that translates directly into dollars.
2. **The client is mid-market and growing.** This model does not work for SMBs (their topline is too small) or for stagnant businesses (the percentage doesn't scale). It works for clients in the $10M-$250M revenue band with year-over-year growth.
3. **The consultant has already delivered a custom project for the client.** The partnership is the upgrade from project to ongoing engagement. It is almost never the first contract.

## The structure

The partnership has four components:

### 1. The budget component

A fixed monthly or quarterly retainer that funds the consultant's ongoing operational commitment to the client. This covers the embedded team time, the regular discovery work, the maintenance of existing systems, and the consultant's strategic availability.

**Sizing.** Typically $10k-$50k per month depending on the depth of engagement and the size of the consultant's embedded team. The budget should be enough to make the consultant whole on the operational cost of the relationship even if no performance fee is ever earned.

### 2. The performance component

A percentage of the attributable result. The mechanic varies by what's being measured:

- **Revenue uplift.** Percentage of incremental top-line revenue attributable to the AI system. Growth-marketing partners conventionally take 15%; AI tech partnerships are still calibrating but commonly land in the 5-20% band depending on how attribution is structured.
- **Cost savings.** Percentage of attributable cost reduction. Often 20-30% of the first-year savings, sometimes structured as a one-time bonus rather than ongoing share.
- **Outcome milestone.** Fixed payment when a defined outcome is achieved (e.g., "$500k when refund rate sits below 12% for three consecutive months").

The choice depends on what the AI system is actually doing and what is most credibly attributable. Revenue uplift works when the system is in the customer-facing stack. Cost savings work when the system is replacing or augmenting internal operations.

### 3. The attribution model

This is the hardest part. The attribution model has to be agreed in advance, written into the contract, and revisited quarterly. Three components:

- **The baseline.** What was the metric doing before the system shipped? Use the 90-day average prior to launch. Document it in the contract.
- **The control.** Some change in the metric will happen for reasons unrelated to the AI system. Industry tailwinds, marketing campaigns, seasonality. The attribution model needs to net these out.
- **The cutoff.** Attribution doesn't continue forever. Most agreements cap performance fees at 24-36 months from system launch, after which the partnership renegotiates.

A typical attribution structure: "The consultant earns 12% of the difference between the actual metric value and the 90-day pre-launch baseline value, multiplied by the metric's dollar value, for 24 months from launch date."

### 4. The exit terms

The contract has to be terminable. Both sides. With notice. Without litigation.

Standard terms: 60-day mutual termination notice, ongoing performance fees vested at the time of termination paid out over the remainder of the attribution window, no clawback on already-earned fees.

## Common failure modes

**Attribution disputes.** The single biggest failure mode. Customer says "that improvement would have happened anyway." Consultant says "look at the baseline." Without a clear attribution model agreed up front, the partnership becomes adversarial at exactly the moment it should be compounding. **Fix:** spend disproportionate time on the attribution model in the contracting phase. Both sides should sign off on the math.

**Wrong metric.** The consultant ties the performance fee to a metric that turns out not to move the P&L, or that moves for reasons that have nothing to do with the system. **Fix:** pick metrics with the most direct possible line to revenue or cost. Refund rate, not "customer satisfaction." Sales-cycle length, not "engagement."

**Misaligned time horizons.** The consultant wants short-term wins to hit the performance fee. The client wants long-term strategic value. **Fix:** structure the budget component generously enough that the consultant is not desperate for short-term wins, and structure the performance component over multi-quarter windows.

**Performance-fee envy from the client's team.** The client's internal team sees the consultant earning seven figures on the partnership and resents it. **Fix:** the performance fee should be discussed transparently with the client's leadership team as the structural shape of the engagement, not hidden in the contract.

**The partnership replacing the project.** Some consultants try to skip the project step and jump straight to a partnership. This rarely works because the trust required for partnership-shaped revenue has to be earned through delivery. **Fix:** see the [Workshop → Blueprint → Project → Partnership Engagement Ladder](/playbooks/workshop-blueprint-project-partnership-ladder).

## Worked example

Custom AI Studio's e-commerce client (the canonical refund-rate engagement):

- **Baseline metric:** 21% refund rate, 90-day pre-launch average.
- **Post-launch performance:** 16% refund rate stable across 3 months.
- **Attributable improvement:** 5 percentage points.
- **Dollar value of 1 point:** $400k annually (based on the client's volume).
- **Attributable annual savings:** $2M.
- **Performance fee at 15%:** $300k annually for 24 months = $600k.
- **Budget component:** $20k/month = $240k annually = $480k over 24 months.
- **Total partnership value over 24 months:** $1.08M.

Compared to a fixed-scope build project that would have closed at $150-300k.

## Why this is the bigger shift than people realize

The reason this model produces dramatically more revenue than time-and-materials is structural, not negotiation skill. AI systems are not really software. They are labor replacement. The value they produce shows up in the client's labor allocation, not their software allocation (see [AI Eats Labor Allocation](/perspectives/ai-eats-labor-allocation)). A consulting firm pricing AI systems as software is pricing against the wrong allocation. Performance-based partnerships price against the labor allocation, which is 50-100x larger.
