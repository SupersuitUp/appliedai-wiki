# Pilot pitch

*How to propose your first engagement so it is almost impossible to say no. One problem, one deliverable, a short clock, a price that makes the risk feel trivial against the upside.*

---

## When to run this

You have identified a real problem in a client's business (often through [workflow decomposition](/playbooks/workflow-decomposition)) and you are ready to propose your first engagement. The instinct will be to swing big. Resist it. Run this playbook to scope the proposal down to something that almost has to happen.

This playbook does not apply when you already have a multi-year retainer relationship with a client and they are asking you to scope a new phase. It applies to the opening move, the first engagement that builds the trust everything else compounds on.

## Prerequisites

- A specific, named problem the client has acknowledged is painful.
- Enough understanding of the workflow to credibly propose a deliverable.
- A draft of what the deliverable would actually do on day one after handoff.

## Steps

1. **Pick one problem.** Not "transform your operations with AI." One specific, painful, measurable problem. Example: "Your team spends 8 hours a week manually reformatting content for different platforms. The pilot delivers a tool that does it in minutes."

2. **Pick one deliverable.** The client should know exactly what they are getting. A working tool. A documented workflow. A prototype they can test on day one. Not a report. Not a strategy deck. Something they can use.

3. **Set the clock.** 2 to 4 weeks. Long enough to ship something real. Short enough that the client does not have to commit to a relationship before seeing whether it works.

4. **Price for low friction.** Keep the price accessible for a first engagement. The math should be obvious. If the deliverable saves the client 8 hours a week at a $100/hour loaded cost, that is $40,000 a year. A $4,000 pilot is a 10x payback in the first year. The math sells itself.

5. **Write the pitch in five lines.** See the template below. Send it in an email or a text message. Do not over-produce this. The trust is already there. You are making it easy for them to say yes.

## What good looks like at the end

- The client says yes within a week.
- The scope is small enough that you can ship it in the stated window without compromising quality.
- Both sides know exactly what done looks like.
- The success metric is unambiguous: either the deliverable does what was promised, or it does not.

## The three rules of a good pilot

**One problem.** Not "transform your operations." One specific, painful, measurable problem.

**One deliverable.** Working tool, documented workflow, or prototype. Not a report. Not a deck. Something they can use.

**Easy to say yes.** Short clock (2 to 4 weeks). Accessible price for a first engagement. So low-risk that no is the harder decision.

## Why small pilots win

Small pilots de-risk the relationship for both sides.

For the client: they are not committing to a six-month contract with someone unproven. They are trying something small. If it does not work, they have lost very little.

For you: you learn how this client communicates, what their real constraints are, and whether you want to work with them long-term. Some clients are great. Some are not. Better to find out in two weeks than six months.

## What happens after the pilot

When you deliver well, the conversation changes. The client stops seeing you as someone who helped with one thing and starts seeing you as the person who understands their business and can solve problems with AI.

At that point they have ten other problems they want your help with. And they start telling their friends about you. That is how a practice grows. The pilot is the entry point to a long relationship, not a transaction.

## The pitch template

A simple structure for proposing a pilot. Send it as an email or message.

> **Problem:** [One sentence describing the pain point you observed]
>
> **Proposal:** I will build [specific deliverable] that [specific outcome]. Timeline: [2 to 4 weeks].
>
> **What you get:** [Concrete thing they can use on day one after delivery]
>
> **Investment:** [Price]. If it does not deliver the value we discussed, we will figure out how to make it right.

Keep it short. Do not over-produce it. The trust is already there. You are just making it easy for them to say yes.

## Common failure modes

- **Proposing too much.** A pilot is not a roadmap. It is one step. Save the roadmap for after you have earned it. Every additional deliverable in the pitch lowers the close rate.
- **Under-pricing to get in the door.** Your time has value. Price fairly for the scope. Clients who will not pay a reasonable amount for a 2-week pilot are not clients you want.
- **Skipping the pilot and going straight to a retainer.** Retainers are earned, not sold. The pilot is how you earn them. Proposing a retainer cold to a client who has not seen you deliver is almost always rejected.
- **Vague success criteria.** "We will see how it goes" is a guarantee of post-engagement ambiguity. Name the metric in the proposal so done is unambiguous.
