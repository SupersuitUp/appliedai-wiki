# Pricing AI engagements

*Price by the transformation, not by the hour. If you price by the hour, you train clients to see you as labor. Price by the value delivered and the conversation shifts from haggling to ROI.*

---

## When to run this

You are scoping an applied AI engagement and you need to set a number. Run this playbook before quoting. Run it again whenever your close rate drifts outside the diagnostic band (see below). Run it especially when the engagement is unusually high-stakes, equity-adjacent, or outside your normal pattern.

This playbook does not apply when the price is fixed by an external rate card (a platform, an employer, a procurement system). It applies to engagements where you set the price yourself.

## Prerequisites

- A defined scope (a [pilot scope](/playbooks/pilot-scope) document is ideal, even a draft).
- Enough discovery to estimate the annual business impact in dollars.
- Honest answers about the client's revenue, decision-maker access, urgency, and complexity.
- Honest answers about your own opportunity cost, excitement, and cash needs.

## Steps

1. **Quantify the annual business impact.** Every engagement maps to at least one of revenue unlocked, cost reduced, time recovered, or risk mitigated. Estimate the annual dollar impact. This is the anchor for the price.

2. **Assess client variables.** Run the client through the variables table. Revenue scale, decision-maker access, urgency, complexity, duration, strategic value. Each variable pushes the price up or down from your baseline.

3. **Assess your own variables.** What is your opportunity cost? Does this energize or drain you? Will it become a case study you tell for years? Is this someone you want in your orbit long-term? Do you need cash now, or can you weight toward equity or revenue share?

4. **Pick the pricing model.** Match the model (project fee, retainer, hybrid, equity or revenue share) to the engagement shape. Most engagements work best as hybrid: project fee for the build, retainer for ongoing value.

5. **Set the number.** Aim for 10 to 20% of Year 1 value as a starting band. Adjust based on the variables. Below the minimum floors (see below) you signal cheap labor and attract the wrong clients.

6. **Check against close rate.** Your close rate is the diagnostic. If almost everyone says yes, you are underpriced. The right price produces some "no"s.

## What good looks like at the end

- A defensible number you can quote without flinching.
- A model (project, retainer, hybrid, equity) that matches the engagement shape.
- A clear way to frame the price as a fraction of value to the client.
- Confidence that the price will produce a healthy mix of yes and no answers, not a 100% close rate.

## The core principle

**Price by the transformation, not by the hour.**

When you say "20 hours at $100/hour," the client focuses on the 20 hours. When you say "this system will save your team $240,000 a year and the investment is $8,000," the client focuses on the ROI. The second conversation closes. The first invites haggling.

## Growth center, not cost center

**Position your work as a growth engine, not an expense.**

Trying to sell AI as a way to cut costs puts you in a losing position. You are a new line item competing against existing ones. It is hard to win a budget fight by being a new cost.

Frame your work around revenue unlocked, customers gained, capabilities built, markets entered. Organizations that see AI implementation as a growth function scope bigger engagements, invest with conviction, and build lasting partnerships.

This reframe changes everything: pricing conversations, proposals, case studies, and the clients you attract.

## The pricing calculator

### Step 1: Quantify business impact

| Impact type | How to quantify |
|---|---|
| **Revenue unlocked** | New revenue streams, increased conversion, faster sales cycles |
| **Cost reduced** | Headcount avoided, tool consolidation, error reduction |
| **Time recovered** | Hours/week saved x loaded hourly cost of the people freed up |
| **Risk mitigated** | Cost of compliance failures, data loss, competitive displacement |

**The formula:** estimate the annual impact in dollars. Your price should be 10 to 20% of Year 1 value. Position this as: "You keep 80 to 90% of the value. I take 10 to 20% for making it happen."

### Step 2: Client variables

| Factor | Lower price signal | Higher price signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Client revenue** | Pre-revenue or small | $10M+ established |
| **Decision-maker access** | Talking to a manager | Talking to the CEO or CTO |
| **Urgency** | "Exploring AI someday" | "We are losing to competitors NOW" |
| **Complexity** | Single workflow | Multi-system transformation |
| **Duration** | One-off build | 4 to 6+ month engagement |
| **Strategic value** | Internal convenience | Core business IP or competitive moat |

### Step 3: Your own variables

| Factor | Question to ask yourself |
|---|---|
| **Opportunity cost** | What am I NOT doing while I work on this? |
| **Excitement** | Does this energize me or drain me? |
| **Case study value** | Will this become a story I tell for years? |
| **Relationship depth** | Is this someone I want in my orbit long-term? |
| **Equity belief** | Do I believe in this company's upside enough to take equity? |
| **Cash need** | Do I need immediate cash, or can I weight toward equity or rev share? |

## Four pricing models

### Model A: value-based project fee

Best for defined-scope builds with measurable ROI.

**How to calculate:**
1. Quantify the annual business impact.
2. Charge 10 to 20% of Year 1 value.
3. Minimum floor: $5,000 for any meaningful project.

**Example:** an AI voice agent for a home services company. They currently close 2 of 10 leads because the other 8 are not responded to fast enough. With the agent, they close 8 of 10. At $4,000 per deal, that is $24,000 a month in recovered revenue, or $288,000 a year. Project fee: $8,000. Less than a single month's value. The client does not blink.

### Model B: monthly retainer

Best for ongoing relationships where the system needs tuning and the client needs coaching.

**How to calculate:**
- For revenue-generating systems: $1,000 to $3,000 a month (a fraction of monthly value created).
- For time-saving systems: 15 to 20% of monthly time value saved.
- Itemize what the retainer includes: monitoring, optimization, coaching, priority support, monthly impact reports.

**Why retainers work:** a monthly impact report showing "47 leads contacted, 13 deals closed, $52,000 estimated revenue impact" turns a $1,000-a-month retainer into the most obvious investment the client makes.

### Model C: hybrid (setup fee plus retainer)

Best for most engagements. Gives upfront cash flow and recurring revenue.

**Structure:** project fee for the build plus monthly retainer for ongoing value.

**Example:**
- $15,000 to $25,000 upfront for discovery and build (3 to 4 months).
- $2,000 to $5,000 a month retainer for ongoing coaching, optimization, evolution.
- Total Year 1 value to you: $39,000 to $85,000.

The hybrid model works psychologically too. The upfront fee makes the client take the engagement seriously. The monthly retainer keeps you engaged and prevents the build-and-ghost dynamic that kills one-time projects.

### Model D: equity or revenue share

Best for strategic partnerships where you deeply believe in the company's trajectory.

**When to use:**
- You genuinely believe the equity will be worth something.
- You like the people enough to be tied to them for years.
- The cash component still covers your costs.
- The work produces reusable case studies or systems.

**Structure:** reduced cash fee plus equity stake or percentage of net profit. Common ranges: 5 to 20% depending on depth of involvement.

### Model E: gain sharing

Best for high-confidence, high-capability work where you can predict and measure the result.

You take an agreed percentage of the gain itself: the savings you uncover or the upside you unlock. It sits above a high retainer on the leverage ladder, because a retainer caps your upside and gain sharing does not. Usually stacked on a smaller base fee. Only reach for it when the gain has a clear baseline, is attributable to your work, and the client will honor an agreed count. Full mechanics, the two shapes, and the preconditions are at [gain sharing](/concepts/gain-sharing).

## The discovery conversation

You do not lead with the price. You lead with the diagnosis. If the client has already completed a [pilot scope](/playbooks/pilot-scope), you can move faster. If they have not, the discovery conversation IS the scoping, and it should be a paid engagement.

### Step 1: diagnose the pain

Questions that reveal the cost of the problem:

- "Walk me through what happens when [problem] occurs today."
- "How many hours per week does your team spend on [manual process]?"
- "What does that cost in loaded salary?"
- "What happens to deals when [bottleneck] slows things down?"
- "How many opportunities have you lost because of this?"

### Step 2: quantify the gap

Reflect the cost back:

- "So you are spending roughly $X a year on this problem today."
- "And the opportunity cost of not solving it is another $Y."
- "That means this problem is costing you about $Z per year."

At this point the client is not thinking about your hourly rate. They are thinking about the $Z they are losing.

### Step 3: position the investment

Frame the price as a fraction of the value:

- "I can solve this for [price], which is roughly [X]% of what it is costing you annually."
- "You keep 80 to 90% of the value. I take 10 to 20% for making it happen."
- "The system pays for itself within [timeframe]."

### Step 4: justify with proof

Reference comparable results when you have them:

- "I built a similar system for [anonymized client] that saved them [X hours a week]."
- "Organizations I have worked with typically see [X] ROI in the first quarter."

If you do not have case studies yet, start with a lower price to get one. Then double on the next engagement. Keep doubling until you start hearing no. If your close rate is 100%, your prices are too low.

## Close rate as pricing diagnostic

Your close rate tells you exactly how mispriced you are. If almost everyone says yes, you are leaving money on the table. The right price produces some "no"s.

| Your close rate | What it means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **35%** | You are priced correctly | Hold steady |
| **40 to 50%** | You have a 25 to 50% price increase sitting there | Raise prices on the next proposal |
| **50 to 60%** | You are underpriced by 1.5 to 1.75x | Significant raise needed |
| **60 to 70%** | You have at least a double available | Double your price on the next engagement |
| **70 to 80%** | You have a 2 to 3x increase available | You are dramatically undercharging |
| **80%+** | Some prospects may not take you seriously | Raise immediately |

At the higher close rates, you are not just leaving revenue on the table. You are actively signaling that you might not be good enough for serious buyers. Mid-market and enterprise clients associate low prices with low quality. A prospect who does not flinch at your price probably expected to pay more.

Test it: raise your price by 50% on the next three proposals and track what happens. Most practitioners discover their close rate barely changes.

## Selling out of your own wallet

The most common pricing mistake: pricing based on what *you* would pay, not what the *client* values.

This is called selling out of your own wallet. It happens because you know how to do the work, so the work feels easy to you. A mechanic would never pay someone to change their oil because they know how. But if your car is broken and you do not know how, you would pay a lot.

The same dynamic applies to applied AI work. You know how to build the system, so it feels like just a few days of work. But the client is not buying your days. They are buying the outcome: leads responded to, hours recovered, revenue unlocked. The value of that outcome has nothing to do with how easy it was for you.

**Signs you are selling out of your own wallet:**
- You feel embarrassed quoting your price.
- You preemptively offer discounts before the client pushes back.
- You think "that is a lot of money" when calculating your fee.
- You have friends in the room making 10x your revenue and you are surprised.

**The fix:** price based on the client's world, not yours. If you save a company $300,000 a year, charging $30,000 is not expensive. It is 10% of the value. The client's CFO will approve that without a second meeting.

## Tiered pricing for scaled services

If your work scales with a measurable client input (ad spend, transaction volume, API calls, seats), consider a tiered model with decreasing percentages at each tier.

**How it works:** set a base fee that covers your cost to serve, no matter what. Add incremental percentages that decrease as the client scales:

| Tier | Example |
|---|---|
| **Base fee** | $6,000 a month (covers setup, management, reporting) |
| **$20K to $100K ad spend** | +5% of spend in this tier |
| **$100K to $200K ad spend** | +2.5% of spend in this tier |
| **$200K+ ad spend** | +1% of spend in this tier |

**Two rules:**
1. **Not retroactive.** Each percentage applies only to spend within that tier. The client's total fee is the base plus the sum of each tier's contribution. The effective percentage decreases as they scale.
2. **Survivorship bias works in your favor.** Clients look at the top tier and see that scaling is almost free. They aspire to get there. Meanwhile the base fee and middle tiers are where your margin lives.

**Why this beats flat fees:** a flat fee punishes you as the client grows. If their volume doubles, your workload increases, but your revenue stays flat. Tiered pricing aligns incentives: when the client wins, you win. The decreasing percentages mean the client never feels gouged at scale.

**When to use this:** any engagement where the client's investment or usage scales over time. Performance marketing, managed AI services, API-based products, seat-based deployments.

## Price floors

These exist because anything below them signals cheap labor and attracts the wrong clients.

| Engagement type | Minimum |
|---|---|
| Discovery or audit | $2,000 |
| 1-month sprint project | $5,000 |
| 3 to 6 month transformation | $15,000 |
| Monthly retainer (coaching plus optimization) | $2,000 a month |
| Enterprise culture transformation | $50,000+ |

## Red flags (walk away)

- They ask for your hourly rate before understanding the scope.
- They compare you to freelancers or Upwork contractors.
- "Can you just build me a chatbot?"
- They want to pay per feature, not per transformation.
- No executive sponsor (a middle manager cannot authorize real spend).
- They want to own everything with no ongoing relationship.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: lead response system for a services company

Marcus, an applied AI engineer, is asked by a regional plumbing company ($3M a year revenue) to build a system that responds to inbound leads within 60 seconds, qualifies them, and routes hot ones to dispatch. Currently 70% of leads go unanswered because the team is on job sites all day.

**The math:**
- 40 leads a month, currently closing 12 (30% response rate).
- With the system: 85% response rate, estimated 34 closeable leads.
- Average job value: $1,200.
- Delta: 22 additional closes a month = $26,400 a month = $316,800 a year.

**Price:** $6,000 project fee plus $800 a month retainer. The project fee is under 2% of Year 1 value. The retainer is 3% of the monthly delta. The owner sees this as a no-brainer.

### Example 2: knowledge system for a manufacturing firm

Priya is building an internal knowledge base with three AI agents for a mid-size electronics manufacturer ($85M a year revenue). The agents handle supplier lookup, compliance document retrieval, and engineering spec search. Engineers spend 8 to 12 hours a week hunting for information across shared drives, email, and tribal knowledge.

**Discovery questions:**
1. What decisions does this knowledge system need to support?
2. Who are the users of the three agents? What do they do today without them?
3. How many hours a week do people currently spend on what these agents would automate?
4. What is the loaded cost of those people's time?
5. Are there revenue implications? (Faster decisions, fewer errors, competitive advantage.)
6. What is the cost of a bad decision that better knowledge access would prevent?

**The math:**
- 15 engineers x 10 hours a week saved x $95 an hour loaded cost = $741,000 a year.
- Plus: faster quoting cycles, fewer compliance errors, reduced rework.
- Conservative Year 1 value: $500,000+.

**Price:** $50,000 project fee (4-month build) plus $5,000 a month ongoing retainer. That is 10% of conservative Year 1 value for the project fee. The VP of Engineering approves it in one meeting.

**How Priya frames it:** "This is not a software build. It is a strategic intelligence layer for your engineering team. The knowledge system becomes institutional memory that compounds over time. The agents turn that memory into action. The question is not what this costs. It is what it costs you to NOT have it for another year."

### Example 3: equity play for an early-stage company

Derek is approached by a Series A startup ($2M ARR, growing fast) to build their entire AI operations layer. He loves the team, believes in the product, and wants long-term upside.

**Structure:** $3,000 a month retainer (covers his time costs) plus 3% equity vesting over 2 years. If the company hits $20M ARR, that equity is worth real money. If it does not, he still got paid for his time. He only takes this deal because he genuinely believes in the company and wants to be tied to these people for years.

## Building your proof

You do not need 50 case studies to start pricing on value. You need one solid proof point.

**Structure your first case study like this:**

- **Before:** client was spending [X hours a week] on [manual process], costing [$Y a month].
- **After:** the system reduced this to [Z hours a week], saving [$W a month].
- **What you did:** built [specific system] connecting [platforms/tools]. Implemented [key features]. Trained the team on the new process.
- **Result:** client recovered [X hours a week] and [$Y annually]. Paid [$Z] for implementation.

One documented before-and-after with real numbers is more powerful than any pitch deck.

Once you start retainers, send monthly impact reports. Show the numbers. You are constantly reinforcing value and justifying the investment.

## Common failure modes

- **Hourly pricing.** You are training the client to focus on effort, not outcomes. Stop.
- **No anchor on annual value.** Without a dollar number for the problem, the price feels arbitrary. Anchor before quoting.
- **Quoting before discovery.** A number quoted before you understand the workflow and the cost of the problem is a guess. Charge for the discovery if needed.
- **100% close rate.** You are underpriced. Raise until you start hearing no on roughly 1 in 3 proposals.
