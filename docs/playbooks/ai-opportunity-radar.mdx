# AI Opportunity Radar

*A brainstorming tool for surfacing AI strategy moves you would otherwise miss. For each AI capability, generate first-order possibilities and second-order possibilities. The second-order column is where wave-3 strategy lives.*

---

## When to run this

The Opportunity Radar is most useful at the front of a strategy engagement, after you have established the wave-3 vision but before you scope concrete initiatives. It is also useful as a periodic re-scoping exercise when a new AI capability lands and the team needs to think past the obvious applications. The tool was introduced by Board of Innovation; this page documents how to use it.

## The structure

Three columns:

1. **AI capability.** A named, current AI capability. Examples: "instant generation of any digital artifact," "near-zero marginal cost of production," "agentic action-taking across software," "empathy simulation from wearable signals," "multilingual real-time translation."
2. **First-order possibilities.** The obvious direct applications of the capability. What does it let you do that you could not do before?
3. **Second-order possibilities.** The cascading consequences. If first-order becomes ubiquitous, what changes in the market, in customer behavior, in business models, in competitive dynamics?

## A worked example

| Capability | First-order | Second-order |
|------------|-------------|--------------|
| AI takes digital actions on your behalf | AI assistants buy products for people | End of brand loyalty as we know it. Purchase decisions become rational rather than emotional. |
| Empathy simulation from wearable data | Personalized experiences tuned to current mood | Fully personalized reality through smart glasses. Shared cultural experience fragments. |
| Infinite feature combinations generated on demand | Better A/B testing | Instant UX. Interfaces generated per user per session. The concept of a "product" loosens. |
| AI tracks outcomes of any action | Better attribution and analytics | Outcome-based business models replace subscriptions. SaaS pricing shifts from access fees to results fees. |
| Zero marginal cost of digital production | Cheaper software, cheaper content | Age of abundance, or a deflationary economy. Cost of goods reshapes consumer behavior. |

The pattern: first-order is easy to generate and usually maps to wave-1 or wave-2 thinking. Second-order is harder, takes more time, and usually maps to wave-3 thinking.

## How to run a Radar session

1. **Pre-work.** Build a candidate list of AI capabilities you want to scan. Aim for 10-15 capabilities. Include both proven capabilities and capabilities that are clearly arriving within 18 months.
2. **First pass, first-order.** Split the room into small groups. Each group takes a subset of capabilities and produces first-order possibilities, one per capability. Time-box to 20 minutes. The constraint is not creativity, it is staying first-order.
3. **Second pass, second-order.** Same groups, same capabilities, but now generate second-order possibilities for each first-order item. Time-box to 30 minutes. Most teams will struggle here. That is the point. Second-order thinking is where the leverage is.
4. **Cluster and theme.** Cross-group, identify second-order possibilities that cluster together. Those clusters point to wave-3 strategy shifts in your industry.
5. **Filter against your wave-3 vision.** Which clusters reinforce the wave-3 vision you already drafted? Which surface gaps? Which suggest you should revise the vision?

## What second-order thinking looks like in practice

Most teams default to first-order even when asked to think second-order. Symptoms: they answer "what could a customer do with this" instead of "what changes about the market if everyone has this." Coach them by asking three follow-up questions for every first-order item:

- If this becomes ubiquitous, who loses?
- What category that exists today becomes irrelevant?
- What new category becomes possible?

A first-order item that survives those three questions and still has only first-order answers is genuinely first-order. Move on. A first-order item that opens up new answers under those questions has been hiding a second-order possibility. Pull it out.

## Common failure modes

**Treating Radar output as a use-case backlog.** The Radar's purpose is to inform strategy, not to staff a roadmap. Second-order possibilities are inputs to the wave-3 vision, not initiatives to ship next quarter.

**Running it once and shelving the artifact.** Capabilities change. The Radar should be re-run on a defined cadence, at minimum once per year, with new capabilities added each time.

**Skipping the cluster step.** Individual second-order possibilities are not strategically useful in isolation. The strategic signal is in which possibilities cluster together. Always do the synthesis step.
